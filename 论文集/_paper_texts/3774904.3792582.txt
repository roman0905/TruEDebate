Abstract
Understanding the intent behind misinformation helps reveal dis-
tinct patterns and underlying motives, enabling more accurate and
efficient detection. However, existing approaches often struggle
to effectively model the interplay of multiple coexisting intents
within claims, and typically require additional LLM inferences to
infer intent for each sample. In this paper, we introduce an intent-
informed concept activation vector-based mixture-of-experts frame-
work, iConMoE, which models latent intents in an unsupervised
manner and predicts the veracity of claims in an end-to-end fash-
ion, without the need for additional LLM inferences. Evaluated on
three benchmark datasets, our method consistently outperforms
both baseline and state-of-the-art models. We also conduct compre-
hensive ablation studies and error analyses to highlight the contri-
butions of individual components and identify key challenges. In
addition, we provide a qualitative analysis of the learned intents
across samples to offer deeper insights into the model’s reasoning.

In this paper, we aim to address the aforementioned challenges by
proposing a method that learns relevant misinformation intents in
an unsupervised manner, without relying on large language model
forward passes during training or inference. Our approach also
models the interplay of multiple coexisting intents within a claim to
capture deeper nuances underlying misinformation objectives. We
introduce IConMoE, an intent-informed, concept activation vector
(CAV)-based mixture-of-experts framework designed for end-to-
end claim veracity prediction. IConMoE captures a latent mixture
of user intents in an unsupervised manner. Concept activation
vectors for each intent category are precomputed offline using user-
provided examples, which obviates the need for expensive LLM
inferences. Importantly, IConMoE is a plug-and-play module that
can be seamlessly integrated into any pretrained model or LLM
backbone to inject intent-aware reasoning.
We employ cross-attention to learn the alignment between po-
tential intent CAVs and the claim representations, while a mixture-
of-experts mechanism models the latent combination of relevant
intents. To ensure stable training and effective expert routing, we in-
corporate a KL divergence loss. Furthermore, we apply a contrastive
loss on the intent-informed CAV representations to promote seman-
tic disentanglement and enhance the distinction between intent
classes.
Unlike prior baselines such as DM-INTER, which require re-
peated intent-specific LLM forward passes with a time complexity
of 𝑂(𝐾× 𝑁× 𝐷), our proposed IConMoE performs a single-pass
encoding followed by lightweight intent alignment, reducing the
overall complexity to 𝑂(𝑁× (𝐷+ 𝐾)), where 𝐾is the number of
intents, 𝑁is the number of input samples, and 𝐷is the number of
decoder layers. This makes IConMoE substantially more efficient
for multi-intent inference at scale.
The key contributions of this work are as follows: 1) We intro-
duce, for the first time to our knowledge, a method for modeling
intents behind misinformation using precomputed Concept Ac-
tivation Vectors (CAVs), thereby eliminating the need for expen-
sive LLM inferences. 2) We propose IConMoE, a novel framework
that leverages a CAV-based mixture-of-experts (MoE) architecture
to effectively learn the appropriate mixture of coexisting intents
within claims. 3) We provide a comprehensive evaluation of our
model across three benchmark datasets, demonstrating consistent
improvements over relevant baselines and state-of-the-art methods.
4) We conduct extensive ablation studies to systematically ana-
lyze and reveal the impact of each design choice and component,
highlighting their contributions to the overall effectiveness of the
architecture in determining claim veracity.

Related Work
Our approach draws on and unifies ideas from four research areas:
misinformation detection, intent-aware analysis, concept-based
interpretability, and mixture-of-experts architectures with routing
and contrastive objectives.
Misinformation Detection. Misinformation detection has been
a central research focus due to its societal impact. The early meth-
ods combined linguistic characteristics, propagation patterns, and
metadata cues using traditional machine learning [2, 3, 22, 24]. With
the advent of deep learning, CNNs and RNNs were adopted to cap-
ture local and sequential dependencies [4, 25, 31]. More recently,
pretrained transformers such as BERT [7], RoBERTa [20], and De-
BERTa [13] have become the backbone of misinformation detection,
enabling fine-grained contextual modeling [12]. Hybrid approaches
incorporating multimodal or social signals [15, 34, 37] further im-
proved robustness. Despite these advances, most approaches treat
misinformation as a binary veracity classification task, without
explicitly capturing the intent that drives misinformation creation.
Intent-Aware Misinformation Detection. Intent plays a key
role in shaping misinformation, as motives such as political manipu-
lation, economic gain, or satire yield distinct patterns of spread and
reception. Prior work modeled user behavior and diffusion dynam-
ics to approximate intent [23, 37, 45], but these are largely indirect.
More structured approaches rely on curated intent taxonomies
[1, 41], which provide useful frameworks but require costly anno-
tation. Wang et al. (2024) [38] introduced intent embeddings from
human-curated categories to enrich semantic features, while Wang
et al. (2025) [40] extended this with graph-based intent semantics
co-modeling. Related work in stance detection and propaganda
classification [31] addresses latent objectives behind the text but
lacks explicit disentanglement of semantic and intent signals. In
contrast, IConMoE learns mixtures of intents in an unsupervised
manner.
Concept-Based Interpretability. Concept Activation Vectors
(CAVs), introduced by Kim et al. [17], provide post-hoc explana-
tions by quantifying the sensitivity of a model to human-defined
semantic concepts. Extensions such as LG-CAV [14] enhance con-
cept learning in low-data regimes, primarily for vision–language
tasks. In NLP, CAV are used to sensitize models towards novel un-
seen concepts not covered in training for hate speech detection [11].
Beyond interpretability, steering methods including Contrastive Ac-
tivation Addition [28], Concept Bottleneck Models [18] and GCAV
[44] show how concept supervision can guide predictions, yet no
prior work has explored using mixtures of CAVs for intent modeling
in misinformation detection.
Mixture-of-Experts and Routing Mechanisms. Mixture-of-
Experts (MoE) architectures enable conditional computation by
activating only a subset of specialized expert modules per input,
scaling models efficiently [10, 33]. Early routing mechanisms such
as top-𝑘gating suffered from load imbalance and expert collapse
[10]. Alternatives like expert choice and dynamic routing alleviate
these issues, often using auxiliary losses to encourage expert diver-
sity [8, 19]. MoEs have also been applied in domain adaptation and
multitask learning by specializing experts by topic or style [33].
Notably, IConMoE aligns gating and experts with interpretable
intent-specific CAVs and employs contrastive and KL-divergence
regularizations to maintain fidelity to human-understandable se-
mantic concepts.

3.1
Intent Dataset Construction
We define a representative taxonomy of six misinformation intents
based on most frequently observed intent types in widely used
misinformation corpora (Politifact and Snopes) and real-world fake
news cases, denoted as I = { political manipulation, financial ex-
ploitation, ideological/religious indoctrination, social/psychological
manipulation, malicious harm or panic, satire/parody misinterpreted
}. Analyses of these datasets show that these intents collectively
cover the majority of misinformation narratives, spanning political,
social and economic dimensions. While additional intents can be
modeled easily in our framework, we intentionally adopt a compact
taxonomy to demonstrate that even a small set of interpretable
intents can result in strong performance gain.
We first construct a small intent dataset using Gemini[6]. For
Positive samples (𝑥+) for intent 𝑖, we prompt model to output
misinformation sample containing that particular intent and for
Negative samples (𝑥−), we prompt model to output factual claims
matched in topic and style but not expressing intent. This yields a
balanced dataset of 200 samples per intent, which are used for com-
puting linearly separable concept vectors. Since CAVs require only
approximate linear separability, prior work [17] demonstrates that
such compact concept datasets are sufficient to effectively compute
CAVs, highlighting the efficiency of the CAV-based approach. Refer
to A.7 for intent vs non-intent based examples.

Computing Intent based Concept Activation
Vectors
To represent intent categories in the embedding subspace, we ex-

directions in the hidden layer embedding space of a pretrained
language model. Each CAV is captured by the discriminative axis
that separates examples aligned with a particular intent from its
contrastive, non-intent counterparts.

3.8

contrastive separation objective that encourages each CAV-guided

different intents apart, avoiding entanglement between, say, ‘social-

intent categories modeled in our framework (e.g., political manipu-
lation, social polarization, financial exploitation). PolitiFact origi-
nally provides six graded veracity labels—true, mostly true, half true,
mostly false, false, and pants-on-fire—which we merge into binary
classes: real (the first three) and fake (the latter three), following
common practice. Snopes comprises fact-checked claims curated
from the official Snopes fact-checking portal and is annotated with
binary veracity labels.
In contrast, GossipCop [34] is considered an intent-diverse dataset.
It contains approximately 11.8K entertainment and rumor-based
news articles labeled as real or fake. For temporal robustness, we
follow the standard setup by training on data up to 2017 and testing
on claims from 2018. Unlike PolitiFact and Snopes, the claims in
GossipCop often center around celebrity rumors or popular culture
misinformation, that does not directly correspond to the intent
categories defined in our framework.
4.2
Baselines
We benchmark IConMoE against diverse misinformation detection
baselines, covering pretrained transformers, event-aware models,
graph-based frameworks, and intent-conditioned methods. All are
trained under identical splits for fair comparison. Section A.3 con-
tains detailed description of baseline models.
Pretrained Transformers: BERT [7] serve as strong text-only
encoders, T5(base/large) [30] is encoder-decoder model evaluated
in finetuned setting while LLaMA-3.1 (8B) [9] is evaluated in in-
context and finetuned modes.
Event- and Emotion-Aware Networks: EANN [39] learns
event-invariant representations via adversarial objectives, BERT-
Emo [45] integrates emotion in BERT and ENDEF [46] applies
entity-level debiasing to mitigate overfitting.
Multi-Source and Structural Models: MDFEND and Gen-
FEND [26] incorporate domain and multimodal information, while
MGIN-AG [43], MSynFD [42] exploit syntactic or graph-based cues
for relational reasoning.
Intent-Based Models: DM-Inter [38] models auxiliary intent
vectors through self-supervised intervention.

and concept activation vectors. Additionally, we conduct qualitative
router analysis under KL and contrastive loss, explore alternative
CAV computation methods, perform layer-wise examination, and
assess the hyperparameter sensitivity of the loss functions.

5.1
Performance of IConMoE Across Intent
Aware and Base Models
To further establish the effectiveness of IConMoE, we evaluate its
integration with a range of base architectures, from large decoder-
only LLMs (LLaMA-3.1 8B) to smaller and larger encoder–decoder
models (T5-base, T5-large). Table 1 reports results on GossipCop,
Snopes, and PolitiFact. We compare four settings: (1) zero-shot in-
context learning (ICL), (2) intent-enhanced LLMs where the closest
intent tokens are concatenated to inputs, (3) standard fine-tuning
of base models, (4) DM-Inter[38]
Across all three misinformation datasets, GossipCop, Snopes, and
PolitiFact, IConMoE delivers consistent and significant gains over
baseline methods and prior intent-aware models. DM-Inter is built
on top of T5-base, across all datasets, IConMoE (T5-base) consis-
tently outperforms DM-Inter. On GossipCop, it improves Macro-F1
from 79.8 to 81.5, on Snopes, IConMoE lifts performance from 64.5 to
67.3 Macro-F1, and on PolitiFact, from 61.3 to 66.2. These gains con-
firm that modeling mixture of intent as structured concept vectors
with experts captures finer-grained semantic variation than DM-
Inter’s single-intent intervention mechanism. For large decoder-
only models, IConMoE also yields significant improvements over
conventional fine-tuning. On GossipCop, it raises Macro-F1 from
46.2 to 65.3 (+19.1 points), while on Snopes it climbs from 58.3 to
67.5 (+9.1), and on PolitiFact from 55.8 to 63.2 (+7.4).
Key insights from the results are: 1) LLaMA-3.1 8B alone performs
weakly on misinformation detection under ICL or fine-tuning, yet
with IConMoE, it reaches competitive performance which shows
IConMoE acts as lifeline for weaker baselines. 2) IConMoE con-
sistently outperforms prior baseline (DM-Inter) across datasets
and model families. This shows that intent mixture modeling and
CAV alignment are more effective than direct intent embeddings.
3) Performance gains on both T5-base and T5-large suggest that
IConMoE scales favorably with model size, but even smaller models
benefit substantially. 4) IConMoE boosts both encoder–decoder and
decoder-only small/large models, validating its general-purpose
applicability.
5.2
Comparison with Diverse State-of-the-art
Baselines
Table 2 presents a comprehensive comparison of IConMoE against
a wide range of existing misinformation detection baselines on
PolitiFact and GossipCop.
On Politifact, IConMoE with T5 large achieves the best perfor-
mance across all metrics, obtaining 0.6874 Macro-F1 and 0.6875
Accuracy. The improvement is particularly clear in class-specific
F1-scores: IConMoE balances detection across real (0.6809) and fake
(0.6939) classes, whereas prior methods (e.g., GenFEND, DM-Inter)
tend to skew toward one class. The advantage here is important:
Politifact contains politically charged claims with subtle intent
differences, where explicit intent embeddings (DM-Inter) or emo-
tional features (BERT-Emo) alone are insufficient. Since the Poli-
tifact dataset deals primarily with political claims, the intents we
define (e.g., political manipulation, social manipulation) are highly
relevant. This strong alignment between dataset content and intent
categories allows IConMoE to capture nuanced intent signals that
improve classification.
On GossipCop, IConMoE achieves strong performance (0.8423
Macro-F1, 0.8870 Accuracy), significantly outperforming most prior
baselines such as GenFEND (0.8367) and MSynFD (0.8051). Gossip-
cop focuses on celebrity-related gossip which does not align with
our predefined intents. Nevertheless, IConMoE still shows strong
performance. This is because the shared representation layer of our
architecture effectively captures undefined intents, allowing the
model to generalize to gossip-specific cases that were not explicitly
covered by our intent taxonomy.
We note that INSIDE [40] reports slightly higher performance
on GossipCop. However, IConMoE outperforms INSIDE’s reported
results on PolitiFact. Direct comparison should be interpreted with
caution, as INSIDE’s codebase and data splits are not publicly re-
leased and differ from both our setup and prior standard parti-
tions. INSIDE employs a complex graph-based reasoning module
that requires explicit graph construction and additional annotation
alignment, leading to higher computational and design overhead.
In contrast, a critical strength of IConMoE lies in its adaptability,
IConMoE functions as a plug-and-play, model-agnostic module that
integrates seamlessly into any pretrained LLM backbone without
supervising external intent signals, handcrafted graphs, or task-
specific adaptations.

pecially model performance on real claim. This supports our hy-

claim. 4) Expert Specialization: Removing expert MLPs leads to
strong drop in the performance. This confirms that modular experts

Significance of shared gate
We observed that the shared gate exhibits two characteristic behav-

to specialist experts, emphasizing intent-specific reasoning. In con-

enriched representation and relies on the generic representation
from shared expert. This trend is crucial: the shared expert cap-
tures intents that lie outside the intent taxonomy. When a specialist
expert is spuriously activated by intent-like tokens, the shared
expert provides a corrective signal. Formally, the model output

6
Conclusions and Future Prospects
In this paper, we propose IConMoE, an intent-informed Concept
Activation Vector (CAV)-based mixture-of-experts model designed
to effectively capture intent mixtures underlying misinformation.
IConMoE leverages precomputed concept vectors to model poten-
tial intents without relying on large language model (LLM) infer-
ences during training or inference, offering a highly efficient and
scalable alternative to existing methods. Through evaluations on
three widely-used benchmark datasets, IConMoE demonstrates
significant performance improvements over both strong baselines
and state-of-the-art models, all while maintaining an end-to-end,
computationally light framework.
In future work, we plan to investigate the interplay between
emotions and intents in determining misinformation veracity. Ad-
ditionally, we aim to extend our approach to multi-modal misinfor-
mation detection, particularly by modeling intent representations
in the image modality alongside those in text.

A.3
Detailed description of Baselines
We compare IConMoE against both general-purpose models and
specialized MD methods. In particular, DM-Inter is our primary
baseline due to its close design goals. Below is a brief description
of each:
• BERT [7] : A transformer-based encoder pretrained via masked
language modeling and next-sentence prediction which is
widely used in text classification.
• T5 (base/large) [30] : A text-to-text transformer that can be
fine-tuned for classification tasks. We include both T5-base
and T5-large to test capacity effects.
• LLaMA-3.1 (8B)[9] : large-scale LLM evaluated in in-context
learning (ICL), intent-augmented (LLM-Int), and fine-tuning
settings. To ensure computational efficiency, we fine-tune
only its final classification layer.
• DM-Inter[38] : A self-supervised intent-intervention frame-
work where generator is trained to output binary intent
predictions, which are sharpened and integrated with news
embeddings for veracity judgment.
• EANN [39] : Learns event-invariant representations using
adversarial training, aiming to reduce event-based biases in
news misinformation.
• MDFEND[26] : Employs a mixture-of-experts paradigm to
account for domain variation in news to maintain cross-
domain robustness.
• BERT-Emo [45] : Enhances a BERT-based model with emo-
tional features to capture affective signals useful in misin-
formation.
• ENDEF [46] : Introduces entity-based debiasing to reduce
the influence of highly frequent entities in news that may
skew detection.
• MGIN-AG [43] : Combines syntactic dependency graphs with
BERT, leveraging graph structure to augment representation
for detection.
• MSynFD[42] : Builds multi-hop syntactic dependency graphs
over news tokens to integrate syntax-aware signals for im-
proved detection.
• GenFEND[26] : Generates synthetic user comment feedback
via LLM prompts, aggregates them, and uses them to enhance
misinfo detection.
A.4
Implementation Details
All experiments were implemented using the Hugging Face Trans-
formers library with AdamW [21] as the optimizer and a cosine
annealing schedule with 100 warm-up steps. We trained for 5 epochs
with a batch size of 8, a learning rate of 1e-6, and weight decay set
to 0.01. Mixed precision (FP16) was disabled to avoid training insta-
bilities, though BF16 can be adopted on supported hardware. We
evaluated our approach across four strong pretrained backbones:
FLAN-T5-Base and FLAN-T5-Large [5], Google/Gemma-7B [35],
and Meta-LLaMA-3.1-8B [9]. For intent-aware modeling, we first
obtained intent embeddings using the all-MiniLM-L6-v2 encoder
[32], and then determined the closest intent for each claim by com-
puting cosine similarity between the claim and intent vectors. All
experiments were conducted on NVIDIA H100 GPUs with at 90GB
memory.

A.6
Hyperparameter analysis
We further investigate the sensitivity of IConMoe to the weighting
of auxiliary objectives, namely the contrastive consistency loss (𝜆)
and the KL regularization loss (𝛽). Figures 3 report the effect of vary-
ing 𝜆and 𝛽in the range [0.2, 1.0] on Snopes and Politifact datasets.
We observe that as 𝜆approaches 1.0, both accuracy and macro F1
consistently improve on Snopes, reaching the highest scores when
the contrastive term dominates. A similar upward trend is visible for
Politifact, where performance peaks around 𝜆= 0.6 before plateau-
ing. These results indicate that the contrastive term is crucial for
stabilizing expert separation by pushing apart conflicting intents. In
contrast, increasing 𝛽has a diminishing effect on performance. On
Snopes and politifact, both accuracy and macro F1 steadily decline
as 𝛽grows, indicating that excessive regularization over-constrains
expert routing.

A.10
CAV generated using other LLMs
To evaluate the robustness of IConMoE to the choice of LLM used
for intent dataset construction, we generate Concept Activation
Vectors (CAVs) using intent samples produced by ChatGPT instead
of Gemini. As shown in Table 12, IConMoE maintains comparable
performance across both CAV sources, with only a marginal drop
when using ChatGPT-based CAVs. This indicates that our frame-
work is not sensitive to a specific LLM and benefits primarily from
the intent-informed CAV formulation rather than the generator
model. Gemini-2.5 produced most consistent and least stylistically
biased examples.
A.11
Varying number of intent
We study the impact of intent granularity by varying the number
of intent categories k. Results in Table 13 show that performance
degrades gradually as k decreases, with the full six-intent setting
achieving the best results. This suggests that a compact yet suf-
ficiently expressive intent taxonomy is important for capturing
diverse misinformation strategies, while fewer intents limit the
model’s ability to disentangle semantic intent signals.
A.12
Ethics statement
This work uses large language models (LLMs) solely to generate
synthetic misinformation examples for offline concept extraction.
These samples are used only to compute intent-specific Concept
Activation Vectors (CAVs) and are never included in model training,
evaluation, deployment, or public release.
All generation is constrained by strict safety and content modera-
tion settings, ensuring that samples remain abstract, non-operational,
and do not reference real events, provide instructions, or target
individuals or groups. Generated data are stored locally and deleted
after concept computation.
The proposed IConMoE framework does not require LLM infer-
ence during training or inference, minimizing risks of unintended
