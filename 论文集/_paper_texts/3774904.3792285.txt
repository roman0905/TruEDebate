Abstract
The rapid spread of misinformation on social media has underscored
the importance of automatic fact-checking. Existing fact-checking
pipelines typically rely on multi-stage frameworks involving ev-
idence retrieval and claim verification. However, these methods
face two major challenges: (1) the retrieval process often introduces
noisy evidence, which compromises the reliability of the final verac-
ity prediction; and (2) the verification models may overlook critical
factual details, resulting in hallucinated conclusions. To address
these issues, we propose a fact-checking framework SLED with
Self-supervised denoising evidence retrieval and LLM-Enhanced
Debate-based verification. In the retrieval stage, SLED leverage
trained verifier to assess credibility and necessity of retrieved evi-
dence, enabling the elimination of noisy evidence. In the verification
stage, SLED prompts the LLM to generate dual-perspective reason-
ing and simulates a multi-agent debate, followed by distillation into
a lightweight model for final veracity prediction. Experiments on
CHEF and HOVER datasets demonstrate that SLED achieves the
state-of-the-art results in complex fact verification scenarios.

Introduction
The rise of social media and large language models (LLMs) has
led to a rapid spread of both accurate and misleading information
online [1, 2, 11, 16, 33, 35]. This has raised interest in automatic
fact-checking, which aims to determine the veracity of claims using
external evidence [6, 19, 57].
Unlike earlier fact-checking methods that rely mainly on the
semantic or linguistic patterns of claims [44, 45], Thorne et al. [38]
introduces a structured pipeline consisting of evidence retrieval
and claim verification, which has been proven effective, as each
stage addresses a distinct subtask and contributes to the overall

reliability of fact-checking. However, two major limitations hinder
current approaches.
On the one hand, in the evidence retrieval stage, existing ap-
proaches [30, 39, 47, 49] often tend to retrieve as much evidence as
possible for multi-hop reasoning, which inevitably introduces noisy
information and may undermine the accuracy of the final veracity
prediction. As illustrated in Figure 1, the retrieved evidence related
to the terms “tower” and “Berlin” significantly influence the final
veracity prediction of the claim. Therefore, effectively retrieving
relevant, accurate, and noise-free evidence with minimal human
annotation is critical for enhancing fact-checking performance.
On the other hand, recent approaches have leveraged the grow-
ing reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs) to
achieve promising results in claim verification [17, 18, 27, 52]. How-
ever, these methods are prone to hallucinations due to the omission
of critical information in the claim, which undermines the accu-
racy of veracity prediction. For example, as depicted in Figure 1,
given the claim, a model may predict SUPPORTS based on partial
evidence indicating Reid’s affiliation with Arista, while ignoring
the temporal scope implied by “two years”, thus arriving at an
incorrect judgment. Therefore, an important challenge in claim
verification is how to fully leverage the retrieved evidence to make
more comprehensive and accurate veracity prediction.
To address the challenges posed by inclusion of noisy evidence
in the retrieval process and the hallucination phenomenon arising
from the neglect of factual details during verification, we propose
SLED, a fact-checking framework with Self-supervised denoising
evidence retrieval and LLM-Enhanced Debate-based verification.
In the evidence retrieval stage, we first train a verifier using the
ground-truth veracity labels of claims, and then leverage it to assess
the credibility and necessity of each retrieved evidence, ensuring that
each piece of evidence independently contributes to truthfulness
verification while also maintaining its necessity, which ultimately
yields an accurate, noise-free set of evidence. In the verification
stage, the supporting and refuting agents independently generate
reasoning steps from their respective stances. They then engage in
a debate by posing questions and challenges targeting each other’s
factual details. A judge agent summarizes the debate process, and
a distilled small model leverages this summary to make the final
veracity prediction.
We evaluate our approach on two challenging fact-checking
datasets, CHEF [10] and HOVER [13]. Experimental results show
that our method achieves state-of-the-art performance, demonstrat-
ing its effectiveness in complex claim verification scenarios. The
main contributions of our works can be summarized as follows:
• We introduce a self-supervised denoising evidence retrieval
model that scores candidate evidence based on credibility
and necessity, enabling high-quality evidence selection.
• We design a debate-guided multi-agent reasoning framework
in which two agents argue from opposing perspectives and
a judge agent determines the verdict. To enhance scalability,
we distill this process into a lightweight model that evaluates
both sides’ reasoning to improve verification performance.
• Experimental results show that our method achieves state-of-
the-art performance on CHEF and HOVER, demonstrating
its effectiveness in complex claim verification scenarios.
2
Related Works
Early studies approached fact verification from a claim-only per-
spective, predicting veracity solely based on linguistic features.
Traditional methods relied on bag-of-words [44], convolutional
neural networks [15], graph-based propagation [45, 50], keyword
cues [12, 29], or speaker stance modeling [32]. However, such ap-
proaches suffer from limited interpretability and poor generaliza-
tion, and often fail against carefully crafted misinformation [48, 54].
Later works incorporate evidence retrieval to address more so-
phisticated misinformation. Hassan et al. [8], Thorne and Vlachos
[37], Vlachos and Riedel [40] formalize fact-checking as a pipeline
of claim identification, evidence retrieval, and verification. Evidence
quality is improved through entity-based retrieval [7], DQN-based
selection [42], autoregressive reranking [14], knowledge integra-
tion [58], and unified evidence enhancement [47]. MRR [56] fur-
ther mitigates intent drift via generative multi-hop retrieval and
hierarchical reasoning. Nevertheless, multi-hop retrieval remains
vulnerable to noisy evidence, which can degrade verification accu-
racy.
For claim verification, prior work explores hierarchical atten-
tion [20], joint representation learning [31], debiasing or causal
inference techniques [54], and dual-view frameworks [46]. Despite
improved reasoning ability, these methods may overlook critical
factual information, leading to incorrect veracity predictions.
With the rise of large language models (LLMs), recent studies
leverage their internal knowledge and reasoning capabilities for
fact verification. Approaches such as ProtoCo [53], HiSS [55], Pro-
gramFC [27], Self-Checker [17], and ARG [9] improve verification
through prompting, claim decomposition, modular reasoning, or
rationale guidance. Other works further enhance performance via
iterative retrieval [41, 56] or multi-agent collaboration [21]. How-
ever, LLM-based methods remain prone to hallucinations when
critical evidence is overlooked.
Despite these advances, existing LLM-based approaches often
suffer from hallucinations due to overlooking critical information,
which undermines veracity prediction. To mitigate this, our method
employs two collaborative agents that engage in debate from dif-
ferent perspectives, effectively reducing hallucination in LLM rea-
soning.
3
Method
As illustrated in Figure 2, the proposed SLED framework consists
of two main components: (1) Evidence Retrieval: a self-supervised
approach evaluates the credibility and necessity of retrieved open-
domain evidence to filter relevant content; and (2) Claim Verifi-
cation: a multi-agent debate process verifies the claim’s veracity
using the selected evidence and generates interpretable reasoning.

3.2
LLM-Enhanced Debate-based Claim
Verification
After retrieving evidence, we propose a multi-agent debate frame-
work to mitigate hallucinations in large language model reasoning
caused by the omission of factual details in the claim. The debate
process is divided into three stages: Argument Stage, Questioning
Stage and Answering Stage. The Questioning Stage and Answering
stage are performed in an iterative manner, with the number of
iterations denoted as 𝑅.
3.2.1
Argument Stage. Given the claim and the evidence retrieved
during the retrieval stage, the supporting agent and the refuting
agent independently construct arguments from supporting and
refuting perspectives, respectively. Specifically, the construction
process of each agent follows a Perception–Planning–Action para-
digm: Perception focuses on identifying key informational elements
within the claim (e.g., entities, dates, events); Planning involves
organizing these elements in conjunction with evidence to form
a structured argumentative path; and Action corresponds to gen-
erating a well-formed natural language statement that supports
or refutes the claim in a logically consistent manner. The prompt
template is included in Figure 3.
3.2.2
Questioning Stage. Upon receiving the response from the
opposite agent, both the supporting and refuting agents increase
the current debate round to 𝑟+ 1. Each agent then identifies po-
tential flaws, logical inconsistencies, or unsupported claims in the
opponent’s reasoning by jointly considering the opponent’s argu-
ment and evidence, and formulates a targeted question intended
to challenge or clarify the opponent’s position. The prompt can be
referred in Appendix B Figure 9.
3.2.3
Answering Stage. In this stage, both agents address their
opponent’s questions by retrieving relevant information from evi-
dence and generating responses tailored to the specific inquiries.
The Questioning Stage and Answering Stage proceed iteratively,
and upon reaching the predifined number of rounds 𝑅, each agent
generates a final concluding statement. The prompt can be referred
in Appendix B Figure 10.
3.2.4
Judge Agent. Following the three-stage debate, we obtain
analysis and reasoning grounded in the claim and evidence. To
determine the final veracity prediction, a judge agent is prompted
to evaluate the debate by assigning scores to the supporting and
refuting agents, denoted as (𝑆𝑐𝑜𝑟𝑒𝑠𝑢𝑝, 𝑆𝑐𝑜𝑟𝑒𝑟𝑒𝑓). The side with the
higher score is selected as the final decision. The prompt can be
referred in Appendix B Figure 11.
While LLMs are proficient at generating coherent reasoning, they
often struggle to align this reasoning with claim labels in classifica-
tion settings [22, 34]. In our debate framework, both supporting and
refuting agents provide detailed arguments. However, relying solely
on an LLM to interpret these arguments and determine the claim’s
veracity often proves inadequate [34]. To address this limitation, we
introduce a training approach that integrates debate outcome eval-
uation with knowledge distillation. Specifically, the LLM judge’s
decision, along with the gold claim label, is distilled into a smaller
model to improve its ability to synthesize and evaluate arguments
from both perspectives.

Experiments
4.1
Dataset and Evaluation Metrics
4.1.1
Dataset. Under the open-domain setting, we evaluate our
approach on two benchmark datasets: the Chinese open-domain
fact verification dataset CHEF [10] and the English multi-hop fact
verification dataset HOVER [13]. The CHEF dataset consists of 9,001
training instances and 999 test instances, each labeled as one of three
categories: SUPPORTS (SUP), REFUTES (REF), or NOT ENOUGH
INFORMATION (NEI). For our open-book experiment settings, we
focus only on instances labeled as SUP or REF. The HOVER dataset
contains 18,171 training instances. Since its official test set is not
publicly available, following [56], we use the development set as a
substitute test set, which includes 4,000 instances comprising 1,126
two-hop, 1,835 three-hop, and 1,039 four-hop examples.
4.1.2
Evaluation Metrics. Following [10], we adopt micro-F1 and
macro-F1 scores to evaluate verification performance on the CHEF
dataset. For the HOVER dataset, we follow the evaluation protocol
of [13], using macro-F1 to assess performance separately on two-
hop, three-hop, and four-hop instances.
4.2
Baselines
We compare our method with two categories of baselines. The first
includes fine-tuned pre-trained language models such as BERT and
RoBERTa, which take the concatenation of the claim and evidence
as input, where the evidence is obtained either by Pyserini toolkit
(selecting the top-5 most relevant paragraphs from the indexed
knowledge base) [21] or by our retrieval method, followed by a
classification layer. The second includes LLM-enhanced approaches:
Direct Prompting uses in-context examples for direct prediction;
CoT Prompting guides LLMs to reason step-by-step; FOLK [43]
converts claims into first-order logic clauses for guided reason-
ing; ProgramFC [27] decomposes claims into sub-tasks handled by
specialized models; and MRR-FV [56] performs iterative evidence
retrieval and multi-hop reasoning via memory tokens and a con-
structed reasoning graph. LoCal [21] proposes a logical and causal
fact-checking framework based on multiple LLM-based agents.
4.3
Implementation Details
All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX A6000
GPU with PyTorch 1.13.1. For evidence retrieval, we use bert-base-
chinese and roberta-base-chinese on CHEF, bert-base-uncased and
roberta-base-uncased on HOVER, with max input length 512, batch
size 16, 10 epochs, and AdamW optimizer (learning rate 2e-4, weight
decay 0.01). For evidence generation, we use Deepseek-V3 [4] and
Qwen-plus [36] on CHEF, and GPT-4o [26] and Qwen-plus on
HOVER. In the verification stage, following LoCal [21], we adopt
gpt-3.5-turbo as base agent. For in-context learning baselines, 20
prompt examples are used as in [28]. For debate evaluation and
content training, we use bert-large with a max length of 1024, and
AdamW (learning rate 5e-5, weight decay 0.01).

Main Results
This section compares the performance of our proposed SLED
model with several baseline methods on the CHEF and HOVER
datasets. The results are presented in Table 1 and Table 2, respec-
tively. Across both datasets, SLED consistently outperforms tradi-
tional retrieval and verification methods. Compared to evidence
retrieved by Pyserini or the multi-hop retrieval method MRR-FV,
evidence retrieved using our method leads to more accurate claim
verification. This is attributed to our multi-source retrieval strategy,
which enhances coverage and diversity, and our self-supervised
denoising evidence retrieval module, which effectively filters out
noisy information. These results demonstrate the effectiveness of
our multi-source evidence retrieval and self-supervised denoising
evidence retrieval framework.
In the comparison of claim verification strategies, we observe
that supervised fine-tuned smaller models outperform LLM-based
direct prompting and chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting on simpler
two-hop claims. This may be due to potential conflicts between the
LLM’s internal knowledge and retrieved external evidence, resulting
in hallucinated reasoning [23]. However, for more complex three-
hop and four-hop instances, LLM-based methods such as DebateFC,
ProgramFC, FOLK, MRR-FV and LoCal demonstrate superior per-
formance on complex claims compared to traditional fine-tuned
models, emphasizing the importance of in-context learning and

4.5
Evidence Retrieval Comparison
Since our approach incorporates search-based and LLM-generated
evidence, comparing directly with gold evidence using F1 scores,
common in previous work, is not sufficiently informative. Instead,
we evaluate evidence quality by feeding evidence obtained through
different retrieval methods into a BERT encoder with a multi-layer
perceptron classifier trained on the CHEF training set. We then com-
pare the resulting classification performance using micro-F1 and
macro-F1 scores, as shown in Table 3. On CHEF, retrieval quality
significantly affects verification performance. Methods using search
engine snippets (e.g., Google Snippets) perform worse than those
using curated documents (e.g., Surface Ranker, Semantic Ranker, Hy-
brid Ranker), indicating the importance of evidence source. Hybrid
Ranker outperforms both Semantic and Surface Rankers, showing
the benefit of combining retrieval strategies. Our method achieves
results closest to using gold evidence, due to multi-source retrieval
and self-supervised filtering. We also conduct ablation studies: w/o
Lcre (removing credibility loss) leads to over 7% performance drop,
indicating the importance of reliable evidence selection. w/o Lnec
(removing necessity loss) causes a micro-F1 drop of 2.82% and
macro-F1 drop of 3.2%, showing that necessity loss helps the model
focus on relevant evidence.

4.6
Ablation Study on Claim Verification
We conduct ablation experiments to examine the impact of key com-
ponents in our verification framework. w/o Debate Evaluation
removes the debate evaluation module and directly uses the LLM-
based judge’s decision as the final prediction. As shown in Table 4,
performance drops across all claim types, with a 4.86% decrease on
two-hop claims. This phenomenon suggests that large language
models may be better suited for reasoning tasks but struggle to
align with discrete labels in classification tasks, whereas fine-tuned
smaller models tend to perform better on simpler classification prob-
lems. However, on three-hop and four-hop claims, performance
remains relatively stable, indicating that LLMs themselves can bet-
ter handle complex reasoning, supporting the effectiveness of using
knowledge distillation to learn from LLM judgments. w/o question
answering disables the questioning and answering between the
two debating agents. Instead, the final judgment is based solely on
their initial statements and evidence. This leads to performance
drops of 5.17% and 5.47% on three-hop and four-hop claims, re-
spectively. The decline reflects that, due to the agents’ comparable
reasoning capabilities, their initial statements alone are often in-
sufficient to differentiate a clear winner. The questioning stage and
answering stage enable deeper interaction, exposing incomplete
reasoning or weak evidence, which is critical for accurate claim
verification in complex cases.

4.7
Parameter Analysis
We conduct an analysis on hyperparameter 𝜆, which regulates the
evidence selection in Equation 7, to examine its effect on both
micro-F1 and macro-F1 performace, as well as on the number of
selected evidence in the CHEF and HOVER dataset. 𝜆is varied
from -0.1 to 0.6. The experimental results in Table 4 and 5 reveal
a clear trend: as 𝜆increases, the number of selected evidence in-
stances decreases. When 𝜆= −0.1, almost all evidence is selected,
whereas at 𝜆= 0.6, the average number of selected evidence items
falls below one. Both extremes result in lower F1 scores, suggest-
ing that the model’s performance degrades when it incorporates
excessive noisy evidence or lacks sufficient supporting evidence.
These findings underscore the importance of appropriate evidence
selection in improving fact-checking accuracy. Additionally, the
CHEF dataset primarily consists of one-hop and two-hop instances,
whereas the HOVER dataset contains more complex cases involv-
ing two-hop, three-hop, and four-hop reasoning. The F1 scores
on HOVER also vary with the number of selected evidence items,
further demonstrating that selecting appropriate evidence is crucial
for evidence-based fact-checking tasks.

4.8
Error Analysis
We further conduct an error analysis by comparing our method
with CoT on 25 correct and 25 incorrect cases for each model. Our
method shows no hallucinations in correct predictions and only two
in incorrect ones. Most remaining errors are caused by evidence
retrieval failures (12 cases), NEI-leaning ambiguity (5), semantic
misunderstanding (5), and reasoning errors (6). In contrast, CoT
already exhibits hallucinations in correct cases (1) and a much
higher number in incorrect cases (8), along with semantic misun-
derstanding (9), NEI-leaning ambiguity (3), and reasoning errors (5).
Overall, these results indicate that the proposed debate mechanism
effectively reduces hallucinations and promotes evidence-grounded
reasoning. Representative cases are provided for illustration.
5
Conclusions
This paper presents SLED, a novel fact-checking framework that
eliminate the noisy evidence in evidence retrieval and enhances
veracity prediction through debate-enhanced reasoning. By incor-
porating self-supervised evidence selection and interactive verifica-
tion with LLM agents, SLED effectively filters noisy evidence and
reduces hallucinations in claim verification. Extensive experiments
on CHEF and HOVER demonstrate that SLED achieves state-of-the-
art performance, highlighting its potential for robust and scalable
fact-checking.
