Abstract
Single-expert fake news detectors, such as Graph Neural Networks
(GNNs) and Large Language Models (LLMs), increasingly struggle
to counter the diversifying camouflage tactics of modern adver-
saries, which range from semantic (e.g., mimicking writing styles)
to structural (e.g., manipulating propagation paths). To address this,
existing methods attempt to build a hybrid model by sequentially in-
corporating GNNs and LLMs; however, such hybridization blurs the
distinction between experts and prevents critical cross-validation.
In addition, existing methods rely heavily on vast labeled data,
which is costly to acquire, particularly for fake news samples. In this
paper, we propose a Semi-supervised Mixture of Experts framework
for Fake news detection, namely S2MOE-F. The core idea of S2MOE-
F is to establish a robust defense against multifaceted camouflage
by cross-validating the complementary judgments of two indepen-
dent experts, GNN and LLM. On the one hand, S2MOE-F drives
experts’ judgments by using a One-Class Classification (OCC) ob-
jective, which constrains true news within a compact hypersphere
and identifies samples outside this boundary as fake, reducing re-
liance on scarce fake news labels. On the other hand, S2MOE-F
generates high-confidence pseudo-labels based on consensus or
divergence between experts to exploit abundant unlabeled data. In
addition, we propose a novel reinforcement learning (RL)-based
routing policy that dynamically determines the dominant expert
for input samples without explicit supervision. Finally, we design a
disentangled masked Transformer to ensure experts’ specialization
by reducing inter-expert redundancy. Extensive experiments on
real-world datasets sourced from Web platforms and social media
demonstrate the superior performance of S2MOE-F.

Introduction
In the era of rapid dissemination of information within social net-
works, the widespread proliferation of fake news has become a
critical concern. For example, the fake news of COVID-19 - the
’consuming highly concentrated alcohol can disinfect the body and
kill the virus’ - led to severe social damage, including but not lim-
ited to more than 5,800 hospitalizations [13]. Therefore, developing
effective fake news detection algorithms is essential to ensure a
more reliable web and protect the public from misinformation. In
response, a variety of advanced approaches for fake news detec-
tion have emerged, primarily centered on Graph Neural Networks
(GNNs) [2, 52, 53] and Large Language Models (LLMs) [12, 43, 44].
To counter these two mainstream detection technologies, fake
news adversaries have correspondingly developed targeted evasion
strategies, as shown in Fig. 1, including: 1) Semantic camouflage:
fake news adversaries could mimic the writing style of true news,
fabricating "citation sources", and even relying on LLMs to craft
textually indistinguishable misinformation [1]. This disguised in-
formation poses a substantial threat to semantic-based LLM de-
tectors [5]. 2) Structural camouflage: on the other hand, fake
news adversaries could manipulate propagation paths through bots,

the structure of social networks. Because GNN-based detectors
are implemented by analyzing network structures, they might be
easily misled by deliberately manipulated propagation paths with
structural camouflage [16]. Thus, single-expert evidence is prone
to bias or deception. The final verdict on suspicious news requires
cross-validating both the semantic and structural evidence.
Several very recent studies have enhanced LLMs’ embeddings
with GNNs to advance detection performance [15, 22, 23, 47]. How-
ever, the primary limitation of these methods is that they dissolve
one expert of evidence into the other by treating LLM embeddings
as simple node features for a GNN, which overlooks each expert’s
role as a distinct line of defense against semantic or structural cam-
ouflage. Such a fusion blurs the distinction between their judgments
and prevents the critical cross-validation required to form a precise
verdict [51]. In contrast, the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) emerges as a
well-suited concept specifically designed to integrate insights from
diverse, specialized experts. Unfortunately, existing MoEs are not
considered a robust defense against both structural and semantic
camouflage. For instance, MIMoE-FND [19] and MisD-MoE [18]
confine MoEs to news content (e.g., text and images), lacking critical
cross-validation against both semantic and structural camouflage.
Furthermore, a fatal limitation of all the aforementioned approaches
is their reliance on supervised learning, which necessitates vast
amounts of labeled data. The extensive manual effort required to
annotate these labels could be costly and labor-intensive, as ex-
emplified by the CREDBANK dataset [25], where over 60 million
tweets for 1,049 events were each annotated by 30 human eval-
uators. Thus, an innovative MoE framework is desired that can
perform cross-validation against both structural and semantic cam-
ouflage while effectively training with a small portion of labeled
data and large amounts of unlabeled data.
To build such a framework, several key challenges still lie in: C1)
Mitigating the severe redundancy between GNN and LLM
experts. The core MoE principle of expert specialization, typically
achieved by assigning experts to distinct data modalities like text
and images [18, 19], is fundamentally compromised when using
GNN and LLM experts that both rely on a shared textual foundation.
This shared reliance, where the same text serves as the LLM’s
direct input and the source for the GNN’s node features, inevitably
leads to significant redundancy between experts. C2) Training the
MoE framework under realistic semi-supervised fake news
detection. MoEs’ reliance on abundant labeled data to jointly train
multiple experts and gating networks severely limits their practical
application [42]. This limitation is acute in fake news detection, as
labeled news is not only inherently scarce but also costly and labor-
intensive to obtain, especially for fake news samples. Consequently,
the crucial and unexplored research problem is to develop an MOE
framework that not only learns with high efficiency from the limited
labeled data but also leverages the diverse insights of its experts to
exploit the wealth of unlabeled data. C3) Developing an expert
routing mechanism with limited supervision. In traditional
MoEs, a gating network learns a routing policy from abundant
labeled data, allowing the final decision to be dominated by the
most suitable expert [36], e.g., the LLM for inputs with structural
camouflage and the GNN for semantic camouflage. However, when
such supervisory signals become insufficient, the traditional gating
mechanism fails to learn this nuanced routing logic. Such a failure
creates a crucial need for a new routing mechanism capable of
learning effectively under limited supervision.
To address the above challenges, this paper proposes a Semi-
supervised Mixture of Experts framework for Fake news detec-
tion, namely S2MOE-F 1. To mitigate inter-expert redundancy
(C1), we design a disentangled masked transformer module, which
disentangles each expert’s embedding into a set of distinct sub-
embeddings. Subsequently, a learnable masked matrix is optimized
via a covariance objective that minimizes redundancy between
sub-embedding pairs from the different experts, thereby enforcing
expert specialization. To enable learning from limited labels
(C2), we employ a One-Class Classification (OCC) objective, which
constrains true news into a compact hypersphere and thereby identi-
fies any sample falling outside this boundary as fake. This objective
is highly suitable for realistic semi-supervised fake news detection,
given the scarcity and high cost of labeled fake news examples.
Meanwhile, we further exploit unlabeled data (C2) through a
pseudo-labeling process, where experts’ consensus or divergence is

Preliminary and Related Work
This section outlines the preliminaries and briefly reviews related
work. A more detailed literature review is available in Appendix A.
Semi-Supervised Fake News Detection. Let D = D𝑙∪D𝑢de-
note the news dataset, where D𝑙= {𝑑1, . . . ,𝑑𝑙} is the subset of
labeled news associated with labels {𝑦1, . . . ,𝑦𝑙}, as 𝑦𝑖= 1 indicates
true news and 𝑦𝑖= −1 indicates fake news. The remaining sub-
set D𝑢= {𝑑𝑙+1, . . . ,𝑑𝑁} contains unlabeled news. The work will
learn a fake news detector, denoted as F , across the entire dataset
D = D𝑙∪D𝑢. The learned detector is a function F : D →R
that maps each news item 𝑑𝑖to a misinformation score 𝑠𝑖, where a
higher score 𝑠𝑖indicates a greater probability that the news is fake.
News Embedding by GNN. Each news item 𝑑𝑖is represented as
a propagation graph G𝑖= (V𝑖, E𝑖), where the node set V𝑖com-
prises the source news item, as well as any interacting users and
linked news articles, and the edge set E𝑖captures interactions (e.g.,
retweets, replies) between them. The GNN expert then encodes
each graph G𝑖into a structural representation vector h𝑖∈R𝑑.
News Embedding by LLM. Each news article 𝑑𝑖∈D is encoded
by LLMs to capture its semantic features. Specifically, we feed the
textual content (e.g., title and content) of 𝑑𝑖into the LLM expert to
obtain a contextualized semantic representation vector z𝑖∈R𝑑.
3
Method
3.1
Overall Architecture
The architecture of S2MOE-F is illustrated in Fig. 3. Firstly, GNN
and LLM experts generate structural and semantic embeddings for
each input news, respectively. These embeddings are then fed into
a Disentanglement Mask Transformer to reduce inter-expert re-
dundancy and integrate into a shared latent space. Then, S2MOE-F
is trained with a comprehensive semi-supervised strategy: a One-
Class Classification (OCC) objective constrains true news into a
compact hypersphere, thereby identifying fake samples as out-of-
boundary points; a contrastive loss ensures stability by preventing
hypersphere collapse; and a pseudo-labeling scheme exploits un-
labeled data via experts’ consensus or divergence. Finally, a novel
reinforcement learning (RL) router learns a dynamic policy to de-
termine the dominant expert for input without explicit supervision.

3.3.1

whereas annotating an item as "fake" is significantly more complex
and costly, often requiring multi-party validation.

Semi-Supdervised Pseudo-Labeling Strategy
To leverage the abundant unlabeled data in D𝑢, we introduce a
strategy that assigns pseudo-labels to high-confidence samples. The
confidence of a sample is determined by the consistency of the GNN

where 𝜽is the center of the OCC hypersphere and 𝜀is a small

A high confidence score signifies strong consensus between the
experts. For top-𝐾high-confidence samples, we then assign relative
pseudo-labels to them:
• Pseudo-True (𝑦𝑖= 1): Assigned to high-confidence samples
where both embeddings are close to the center 𝜽.
• Pseudo-Fake (𝑦𝑖= −1): Assigned to high-confidence sam-

• Low-Confidence: Samples with a low confidence score,
indicating experts’ divergence on their distance to the center,
are not assigned a pseudo-label.
Finally, these newly high-confidence pseudo-labels are added to
the labeled set D𝑙for subsequent rounds of iterative training.
3.5
RL-based Routing Mechanism
As defined in our OCC objective in Eq. (6), the weights 𝑤GNN and
𝑤LLM modulate the respective dominance of the GNN and LLM
experts. A static weighting scheme is suboptimal, as different sam-
ples may require different dominant experts. However, learning a
per-sample routing policy is also unreliable in our semi-supervised
setting. This is because the routing policy is guided by the model’s
outputs rather than by ground-truth labels. Consequently, an erro-
neous judgment from an expert on a single sample can mislead the

Experiment
In this section, we conduct extensive experiments on five datasets
to evaluate S2MOE-F, including ablation studies for module ef-
fectiveness, case studies to validate redundancy removal by the
disentangled masked transformer, parameter sensitivity analysis,
and evaluations of running time and memory usage.
4.1
Experimental Setup
4.1.1
Datasets. GossipCop, PolitiFact, COVID-19, and Weibo-21,
are four real-world fake news datasets, and CTI is a news dataset
generated by LLM. GossipCop and PolitiFact both come from Fak-
eNewsNet [38]. GossipCop mainly covers entertainment gossip,
while PolitiFact covers statements or reports on political topics in
the US. COVID-19 [50] includes news reports and health statements
related to the pandemic, and Weibo-21 [28] includes news, user
posts, and interactions collected from the Weibo platform, CTI [5]
includes various types of news generated by LLM.
4.1.2
Baseline & Experimental Settings. To demonstrate the effec-
tiveness of S2MOE-F, we compare it with several baselines, in-
cluding: BERT [7], XLNet [54], OCC [34], UFD [53], GCNFN [26],
GNN-CL [11], GNN [49], GAMC [59], ARG [12], InfOp [47], and
MFCL [6]. To follow a standard semi-supervised setting [10, 20, 27],
we utilize only 30% of the data with labels, while the remaining
training data and the entire test set are treated as unlabeled. We
report the average results and standard deviations across the four
most commonly used metrics under 5-fold cross-validation. Con-
sequently, both S2MOE-F and all baselines are trained in the same
semi-supervised setting. More details of the experimental setting,
including baseline summary and parameter settings, are shown in
the Appendix C.1.
In our experiments, we further evaluate S2MOE-F with two
different LLMs as the semantic backbone: Llama3 and Qwen. The
version using Llama3 is denoted as S2MOE-FL, while the one using
Qwen is denoted as S2MOE-FQ. Among them, S2MOE-FL serves as
our primary model, as it achieves superior performance across all
evaluation metrics.

4.3
Ablation Study
In this section, we demonstrate the necessity of each module in
S2MOE-F by conducting ablation studies. We use S2MOE-FL in ab-
lation studies due to its better performance, where we observe the
model’s performance after removing each module to assess their
impact on S2MOE-FL:
1) S2MOE-FL-⊘GNN removes the GNN and trains the model only
on text data; 2) S2MOE-FL-⊘Llama removes the LLM and trains the
model only on graph-structured data; 3) S2MOE-FL-⊘RL removes
the RL-based router, when calculating OCC loss, fixing the weights
of GNN and LLM; 4) S2MOE-FL-⊘LOCC removes the OCC loss
and only depends on the contrast and covariance losses to opti-
mize the model; 5) S2MOE-FL-⊘LCov removes the covariance loss
and uses two fixed mask matrices when disentangling; 6) S2MOE-
FL-⊘LInfoNCE removes contrastive loss and depends on OCC and
covariance losses to evaluate model performance.
Table 2 demonstrates that the complete S2MOE-FL achieves the
best overall performance across all datasets, highlighting the ne-
cessity of each component. S2MOE-FL-⊘GNN shows a significant
drop in performance, indicating that removing the GNN leads to
a loss of propagation structure information, which is critical for
misinformation detection. S2MOE-FL-⊘Llama also causes a notable
decline, highlighting the importance of semantic understanding
provided by the LLM. When the RL-based router is removed, perfor-
mance consistently decreases, showing that dynamically balancing
the contributions of GNN and LLM is beneficial for improving
performance. Without OCC loss, depending only on assessing simi-
larities and differences between news is insufficient to effectively
distinguish true and fake news, while excluding the covariance loss
degrades performance on datasets, confirming its role in disentan-
gling redundant features. Moreover, removing contrastive learning
loss increases the risk of hypersphere collapse, further emphasizing

4.5
Parameter Analysis
In this section, we analyze the parameters to ensure the best per-
formance of S2MOE-F. Fig. 5 presents the parameter sensitivity
analysis of 𝜆, 𝜇, 𝛾, and batch size on the COVID-19 dataset, where
𝜆, 𝜇, and 𝛾correspond to the weights of the OCC loss (LOCC),
contrastive loss (LInfoNCE), and covariance loss (LCov) in Eq. (8),
respectively. As shown in the figure, the model achieves the best
overall performance when 𝜆, 𝜇, and 𝛾are set around 0.5. Specifi-
cally, as 𝜆increases from 0.1 to 0.5, all evaluation metrics (AUC,
F1, Recall, and Precision) consistently improve, indicating that a
moderate weighting of LOCC effectively enhances the model’s ca-
pability to distinguish fake news. However, further increasing 𝜆
beyond 0.5 leads to a slight performance decline, as overempha-
sizing LOCC reduces the contribution of auxiliary objectives. For
𝜇, which controls the contrastive loss LInfoNCE, the performance
gradually decreases as 𝜇grows, suggesting that a small coefficient
is sufficient to prevent hypersphere collapse. Similarly, for 𝛾, which
regulates the covariance loss LCov, the model performs best when
𝛾is within the range of 0.3– 0.5. This implies that LCov effectively
assists the disentanglement module by guiding the learning of two
mask matrices, while excessive weighting reduces feature separa-
bility. Finally, we analyze the impact of batch size. As shown in the
last subplot of Fig. 5, increasing the batch size generally improves
the performance up to 32, where all metrics reach their peak. Batch
sizes larger than 32 lead to a slight decrease in some metrics, which
suggests that too large a batch size may reduce the generalization
ability of the model.

different metrics. Transformer-based baselines such as BERT and
XLNet exhibit much higher time and memory consumption (over
600 ms per step and 2×103 MB) yet fail to achieve competitive per-
formance, highlighting their limited efficiency in this task. Simpler
GNN variants (e.g., GCN-FN, GNN-CL, GNN) are faster but less
accurate. Overall, S2MOE-F provides the best balance between per-
formance, training speed, and memory footprint, confirming the
advantage of MoE.
5
Conclusion
In this paper, we propose S2MOE-F, a semi-supervised mixture-of-
experts (MoE) framework for fake news detection that leverages
the complementary strengths of the Graph Neural Network (GNN)
and Large Language Model (LLM) experts. Our approach mitigates
inter-expert redundancy with a disentangled masked Transformer,
employs an One-Class Classification (OCC) objective and pseudo-
labeling to address label scarcity, and introduces an RL-based router
for dynamic expert weighting. Extensive experiments on real-world
datasets demonstrate that S2MOE-F achieves superior performance
and provides a robust defense against both semantic and struc-
tural camouflage, even with few labels. These results highlight a
promising direction that uses MoE frameworks for realistic semi-
supervised fake news detection.

A
Related Work
Context-based Fake News Detection. There are some mature
methods for fake news detection based on text content, for ex-
ample, a recent work proposed a deep multiple instance learning
framework, Conv-ADMIL [14], which integrates attention mecha-
nisms with contextual neural language models, which enhanced the
model’s ability to distinguish between true and false news. Some
other methods detect fake news in literature by capturing the style
characteristics of fake news [31] and factual reasoning [37, 45].
Wu et al. [46] proposed a multimodal fusion method that used a
collaborative attention network to integrate text and visual infor-
mation to improve the detection accuracy. Dun et al. [9] developed
KAN, which incorporated external knowledge into the attention
mechanism to improve the model’s understanding of context. Sing-
hal et al. [39] introduced SpotFake+, which combined textual and
visual information through transfer learning to provide more pow-
erful detection results. These studies highlight the effectiveness of
content-based approaches for detecting fake news, demonstrating
their ability to capture semantic cues indicative of misinformation.
Graph-based Fake News Detection. In recent years, using graph
structures to detect fake news has attracted wider attention [57].
The ability of GNN to model complex relations demonstrates promis-
ing progress in this field. GCAN [21] achieves interpretable fake
news detection by combining the graph structure and the collab-
orative attention mechanism. Bian et al. [2] used a bi-directional
graph convolutional network to effectively capture the complexity
of information interaction on the social network for fake news
detection. Nguyen et al. [29] designed the FANG model, which used
graph representation learning methods to strengthen the support of
the social context for fake news detection. Min et al. [24] developed
the Post-User Interaction Network, which adopted a divide-and-
conquer strategy to accurately model the interactive relationship
between users and posts. In addition, Phan et al. [30] conducted a
comprehensive review of fake news detection methods based on
graph neural networks, summarizing the advantages and applica-
tion scenarios of current technologies. These studies demonstrate
that graph-based approaches not only improve the accuracy of
fake news detection systems but also enhance their interpretabil-
ity. However, current methods still have many drawbacks. Most of
them rely on supervision and a single view.
One Class Classification. One-Class Classification (OCC) is a
powerful technique for detecting anomalies, particularly useful
when only true news or very limited fake news is available. Ruff
et al. [34] introduced Deep One-Class Classification, using deep
neural networks to map data into a compact latent space, enhanc-
ing generalization. Chalapathy et al. [4] combined autoencoders
with one-class Support Vector Machine (SVM) to detect anom-
alies in high-dimensional data. Qiu et al. [32] extended this to
graph-level anomaly detection [55, 58, 60–62] by integrating GNNs
with OCC techniques, effectively capturing structural anomalies in
graphs. These advancements demonstrated the potential of deep
learning and graph-based methods in handling complex and high-
dimensional data, providing robust solutions for various anomaly
detection tasks. OCC methods typically aim to learn a decision
boundary that separates normal data from potential outliers by
enclosing the normal data points in a compact hypersphere space.

B.2
Hypersphere Collapse Prevention
Hypersphere collapse is a trivial optimum of the Eq. 6 objective.
When all feature vectors are mapped to the center 𝜽, the radius of
the hypersphere becomes zero. We first prove that when all feature
extractors are constant, the OCC model is prone to hypersphere
collapse. Then, we show that incorporating contrastive learning
can prevent this hypersphere collapse.
Proposition B.1. If the feature extractor is constant, all extracted
˜h𝑖and ˜z𝑖are equal to 𝜽, In this case, LOCC attains its minimum
value of 0, leading to hypersphere collapse.
Proof. LOCC is the squared ℓ2 norm of the distance between
˜h𝑖, ˜z𝑖and 𝜽, when ˜h𝑖= 𝜽, ˜z𝑖= 𝜽, all embeddings converge to the
same point 𝜽, making the distance between ˜h𝑖, ˜z𝑖and 𝜽equal to 0.
Thus, LOCC = 0.
□
In contrast, we combine the OCC loss of S2MOE-F with the
contrastive learning loss. The following proof demonstrates that
setting a parameter does not minimize the comprehensive loss,
thereby helping to avoid hypersphere collapse.
Proposition B.2. If a certain parameter setting exists, then under
the GNN and LLM settings, the constant feature extractor with ˜h𝑖and
˜z𝑖= 𝜽cannot make loss equal to 0.
Proof. When ˜h𝑖= 𝜽, ˜z𝑖= 𝜽, the similarity between ˜h𝑖and ˜z𝑖
is 1, that is cos( ˜h𝑖, ˜z𝑖) = 1. In this case, the maximum value of
contrastive loss is 𝑁log 𝑁, that is, LInfoNCE = 𝑁log 𝑁, at the same
time LOCC = 0. When integrating the two losses, a parameter 𝜇
is introduced so that the loss is 𝜇𝑁log 𝑁, effectively preventing
hypersphere collapse. Furthermore, integration of GNN and LLM
within the OCC module enhances the diversity of news informa-
tion and reduces the noise that might arise from a single view.
Contrastive learning can also align embeddings across GNN and
LLM, and adjust the embedding into a common pattern, making the
embeddings more uniform and preventing news from concentrating
on the hypersphere surface.
□
Proposition B.1 and B.2 demonstrate that our method is not prone
to hypersphere collapse. That LInfoNCE < 𝑁log 𝑁in Proposition
2 can be verified by training the model and evaluating the loss in
the training data. In all scenarios we handle, LInfoNCE < 𝑁log 𝑁
is satisfied after training. By combining LOCC with LInfoNCE, our
method avoids the degenerated solution where all embeddings
collapse to a single point in a high-dimensional space.
B.3
Time Complexity Analysis
For each news item 𝑑𝑖, the overall time complexity of S2MOE-F
is O(|E| + |V| + 𝐾2𝑑+ 𝑁𝑑), where |V| and |E| denote the num-
bers of nodes and edges in the user–news graph, 𝐾is the number
of sub-embeddings, 𝑑is the embedding dimension, and 𝑁is the
batch size. Specifically, the GNN expert requires O(|E|) for mes-
sage passing, while the LLM expert costs O(|V|) per batch for tex-
tual encoding. The Disentangled Masked Transformer introduces
O(𝐾2𝑑) due to cross-view attention among 𝐾sub-embeddings.
The OCC and InfoNCE objectives incur O(𝑁𝑑), and the covari-
ance loss adds a minor O(𝑁𝑑2) term. The RL-based router and
pseudo-labeling components have lightweight costs of O(𝑁) and
O(𝑁log 𝑁), respectively. Overall, the per-epoch training complex-
ity is O(|E| + |V| + 𝐾2𝑑+ 𝑁𝑑), which scales linearly with the data
size. During inference, the complexity reduces to O(|E|+|V|+𝐾2𝑑)
since RL routing and pseudo-labeling are omitted.

C.3
Effectiveness of RL-based Routing during
Inference
To evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed RL-based routing strat-
egy during inference, we conduct experiments on three datasets,
including COVID-19, GossipCop, and PolitiFact. In these three
datasets, we compared our RL-router against the conventional fixed
weights strategy during the inference stage to conduct a direct and
rigorous performance comparison. As reported in Table 4, the RL
router achieves comparable or slightly improved performance in
terms of both AUC and F1 score across all evaluated benchmarks.
In particular, on the COVID-19 dataset, the RL router shows a
modest yet stable improvement over the fixed-weight scheme, sug-
gesting that adaptive routing can better accommodate variations
in inference-time data distributions. Similar trends are observed
on GossipCop and PolitiFact, where the RL-based routing strategy
consistently outperforms the fixed-weight baseline with marginal
yet consistent gains in the core AUC and F1 metrics. These results
indicate that dynamically adjusting routing decisions at inference
time remains beneficial, and provides a more flexible and robust
strategy than relying on static weights that are pre-determined and
unchangeable during inference.
