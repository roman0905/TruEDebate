Abstract
The prevalence of fake news on social me-
dia demands automated fact-checking sys-
tems to provide accurate verdicts with faith-
ful explanations.
However, existing large
language model (LLM)-based approaches ig-
nore deceptive misinformation styles in LLM-
generated explanations, resulting in unfaith-
ful rationales that can mislead human judg-
ments. They rely heavily on external knowl-
edge sources, introducing hallucinations and
even high latency that undermine reliabil-
ity and responsiveness, which is crucial for
real-time use.
To address these challenges,
we propose REason-guided Fact-checking
with Latent EXplanations (REFLEX), a self-
refining paradigm that explicitly controls rea-
soning style anchored on verdict. REFLEX
utilizes self-disagreement veracity signals be-
tween the backbone model and its fine-tuned
variant to construct steering vectors, naturally
disentangling fact from style. Experiments on
the real-world dataset show REFLEX achieves
state-of-the-art performance under LLaMA-
series models with only 465 self-refined sam-
ples. Moreover, owing to its transferability,
REFLEX yields up to a 7.54% gain on in-the-
wild data. Our results further demonstrate that
our method effectively mitigates faithful hal-
lucination, thereby guiding the model toward
more accurate verdicts than previous works in
explainable fact-checking.

Introduction
The rapid spread of fake news on social me-
dia poses a critical challenge to automatic fact-
checking (AFC) (Guo et al., 2022). Recent AFC
systems have increasingly incorporated powerful
Large Language Models (LLMs) to verify claims
and generate explanations. However, the most di-
rect human needs are often ignored. As shown
in Figure 1, even when LLMs produce correct

verdicts, their unfaithful explanations mislead hu-
man judgment. Such unfaithfulness emerges from
deceptive styles in LLM-generated misinforma-
tion (Turpin et al., 2023; Chen and Shu, 2023),
also known as faithfulness hallucination (Huang
et al., 2025a): explanations may begin with factual
statements, but gradually drift into verdict inconsis-
tency, intra-logical contradiction, or even overcon-
fident fabrication. This motivated us to disentangle
fact from deceptive style in explanations. We argue
that in worst cases, these deceptive explanations
cascade into incorrect verdicts, as LLMs construct
holistic internal conceptual representations before
producing rationalized outputs rather than strictly
mirroring next-token-prediction (Anthropic, 2025).
While these unfaithful failures can be traced
back to internal misalignments, existing AFC meth-
ods like HiSS (Zhang and Gao, 2023), and L-
Defenses (Wang et al., 2024), mainly rely on ex-
ternal interventions. Although they reduce costly
manual involvement, their heavy reliance on exter-
nal closed-source models or search engines API
introduces extra latency and risks injecting misin-
formation or even hallucinations (Gekhman et al.,
2024) into fine-grained explanations.
To address the above issues, we instead im-
prove faithfulness via controlling model inter-
nal activations.
Our recipe anchors on self-
disagreement veracity signals across models train-

IVs capture style-dependent veracity gains emerg-

Amplifying such signals directly aligns explana-

we adaptively activate the optimal vector based on
probability gaps and leverage it to refine explana-
tions, thereby establishing our self-refining AFC
paradigm, termed REason-guided Fact-checking
with Latent EXplanations (REFLEX).
Experimental results demonstrate that our in-
ternal activation-based methods outperform ap-
proaches relying on external resources either in
verdict accuracy or in both explanation quality with
faithful and concise style. Meanwhile, it can gen-
eralize across backbones, datasets, and combina-
tions with limited samples, suggesting transferabil-
ity (up to 7.54% macro-F gain), flexibility (up to
7.57% macro-F gain), and data efficiency. Addi-
tionally, evidence-free training adapts better to our
paradigm, as evidence also introduces noise and
can amplify hallucinations. Moreover, we observe
a divergence amplification effect: a larger direc-
tion discrepancy between KVs and IVs produces
more effective disentanglement. Further post-hoc
analysis shows that explanations produced by RE-
FLEX are more logically sound and less mislead-
ing, thereby mitigating faithful hallucination and
further improving verdict accuracy.
Overall, our main contributions are as follows:
• We propose REFLEX, a straightforward yet
effective self-refining paradigm for distin-
guishing fact from style, enhancing faithful
reasoning and mitigating misinformation.
• REFLEX achieves state-of-the-art perfor-
mance on the real-world dataset with a small
number of self-refined training samples, while
producing high-quality explanations.
• We show that REFLEX supports cross-model
transfer and exhibits performance-adaptive
transferability, which helps decrease the re-
sponse latency in fact-checking.
• We uncover the divergence amplification ef-
fect in REFLEX, where larger direction diver-
gence yields larger performance gains.

Background
2.1
Faithfulness on NLP
In NLP studies, faithfulness is typically viewed
from two perspectives. The first concerns con-
sistency between model predictions and their un-
derlying reasoning (Jacovi and Goldberg, 2020;
Atanasova et al., 2023; Parcalabescu and Frank,
2024). However, recent work (Yan et al., 2025)
suggests that probabilistic models may not capture
true reasoning, but instead learn superficial patterns
(i.e., style). Therefore, we use human-written ex-
planations as learning objectives to better mimic
human reasoning style. The second focuses on con-
text consistency, often referred to as faithfulness
hallucination (Huang et al., 2025a), including in-
struction, context, and logical inconsistencies. Our
study follows this line.
Recent study in fact-checking (Kim et al., 2024)
treats explanation-evidence alignment as faithful-
ness within context consistency and mitigates it via
multi-agent systems. However, to avoid additional



===== PAGE 3 =====
hallucinations introduced by external evidence and
iterative interaction, we adopt a more aggressive
evidence-free, single-model approach1.
2.2
Explainable Fact-Checking
With the advent of LLMs, recent works have ex-
plored automatic explanation mechanisms to re-
duce human annotation effort on explainable fact-
checking. As shown in Figure 2, HiSS (Zhang
and Gao, 2023) decomposes complex claims into
atomic ones and verifies them via RAG (Lewis
et al., 2020), using retrieved reasoning trajectories
as explanations. L-Defense (Wang et al., 2024)
distills adversarial evidence and explanations from
teacher models. RAV (Shukla et al., 2025) builds a
fully open-source multi-agent dialogue system to
verify claims and treats LLMs’ responses as expla-
nations.
Although these automatic methods signify a
substantial contribution, they face several key
challenges: RAG-based methods can introduce
hallucinations in both retrieval and generation
stages (Qian et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2025). Distilla-
tion and fine-tuning may amplify the snowballing
effect (Leike, 2022) from teacher models’ halluci-
nations. Multi-agent systems suffer from hallucina-
tions spread (Jain et al., 2025; Liang et al., 2026),
and increase interactive latency, making them sub-
optimal for time-sensitive fact-checking. To tackle
these challenges, our REFLEX introduces a self-
refining and transferable paradigm to mitigate hal-
lucination in explanation, reduce inference latency,
and enhance model internal interpretability.
2.3
Style Control in Fact-Checking
Several works improve fact-checking via anchor-
ing style difference across claim sources (human
vs. machine) to enhance verdict prediction (Pérez-
Rosas et al., 2018; Rashkin et al., 2017). How-
ever, humans can deliberately shift style to deceive,
weakening such signals (Schuster et al., 2020; Wu
et al., 2024). In contrast, we optimize models an-
choring on verdict prediction, aligning less decep-
tive explanations with the verdict.
Our approach builds on style control, evolving
from controllable text generation (Dathathri et al.,
2019; Li et al., 2022) to activation editing (Li et al.,
2023a; Hernandez et al., 2023). Recent steering-
vector methods adopt contrastive thinking: ITI (Li

et al., 2023b) identifies CCS (Burns et al., 2023)
proposed contrastive directions of truth at inference
time. CAA (Rimsky et al., 2024) learns stylistic
behavior directions from contrastive pairs. How-
ever, in explainable fact-checking, style and fact
are entangled: longer explanations and the corre-
sponding verdicts often disagree due to attention
dispersion (Wei et al., 2025), making strict con-
trastive pairing infeasible. To address this, we
refine explanations via cross-stage sample selec-
tion and decomposing SVs into KVs and IVs. To
prevent shortcut learning only on fact signals, label-
free interventions are adopted.
3
Methodology
3.1
Task Formulation
Given a dataset D = {(c, evi, v, exp)i}N
i=1, where
c denotes a claim, evi optional evidence, v the
gold veracity label, and exp a human-written ex-
planation, REFLEX aims to reduce deceptive ex-
planations ˆ
exp to align with the predicted verdict
ˆv, thereby mitigating faithfulness hallucination.
As shown in Figure 3, REFLEX operates in three
stages: (1) Dialogue-style Fact-Checker Train-
ing reformulates fact-checking as a dialogue pro-
cess, training LLMs to jointly generate verdicts
and explanations; (2) Adaptive Sample Selection
identifies self-disagreement training samples be-
tween the backbone and its fine-tuned variants; (3)
Self-Explanation Guided Steering (S-EGS) dis-
entangles SVs to KVs and IVs from these samples
and explicitly aligns explanations with verdicts.
3.2
Dialogue-style Fact-Checker Training
3.2.1
Data Preprocessing
Since LLM backbones already encode extensive
factual knowledge (Li et al., 2023b), and fine-
tuning with limited data primarily serves to activate
this knowledge while adapting the model to the tar-
get task (Berglund et al., 2023; Ghosal et al., 2024;
Ren et al., 2024), we aim to better activate the
knowledge embedded in the backbone. Accord-
ingly, we formulate the traditional document-based
training as a single-turn QA-style dialogue. This
design is motivated by prior studies that QA-style
supervision yields stronger knowledge generaliza-
tion during fine-tuning, whereas document-based
supervision (e.g., Wikipedia) leads to weaker gen-
eralization (Zhao et al., 2025).

3.2.2
Training Protocol
Following prior dialogue-based training, we opti-
mize a standard cross-entropy objective to jointly
predict verdicts and explanations. Unlike conven-
tional settings, for comprehensive investigation, we
consider four input-output (x-y) configurations:
x = [c] →y = [v],
x = [c] →y = [v; exp],
x = [c; evi] →y = [v],
x = [c; evi] →y = [v; exp].
To promote coherent reasoning, we incorporate
Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022), in-
structing the model to output its reasoning path as
the explanation, which has been shown to improve
performance (Lippmann and Yang, 2025). Further,
we adopt role-play prompting to enhance reason-
ing (Kong et al., 2024). The prompt templates and
training details are provided in Appendix B and C.
3.3
Adaptive Samples Selection
3.3.1
Self-Knowledge Extraction
After training, we run inference on the training
set using both the backbone and post-trained mod-
els. Since the backbone lacks instruction-following
ability (Liu et al., 2022b), we adopt few-shot
prompting to elicit its knowledge. To prevent data
leakage, few-shot examples are cross-selected from
another training split or drawn from their own held-
out validation set. To mitigate majority bias in
few-shot learning, we balance labels when filling
the maximal context window. For reproducibility,
the temperature is fixed to zero.
3.3.2
Cross-Stage Sample Selection
Given veracity predictions ˆvbase, ˆvsft in training set,
we classify samples anchoring on gold label vgold:
Quadrant 2: ˆvbase ̸= vgold, ˆvsft = vgold
⇒Reasoning Gain,
Quadrant 4: ˆvbase = vgold, ˆvsft ̸= vgold
⇒Knowledge Loss.
As shown in Figure 3, samples in Quadrant 2
(Q2) indicate cases where post-training corrects the
backbone’s verdict due to the enhanced reasoning,
as post-training on limited samples mainly activates
backbone knowledge (Li et al., 2023b) and enables
the model to learn task-specific styles (Berglund
et al., 2023; Ghosal et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024).
Quadrant 4 captures the opposite, where post-
training introduces contradicting knowledge and
causes factual drift, leading to alignment tax (Leike,
2022) and hallucinations (Gekhman et al., 2024;
Huang et al., 2025b). Hence, we adaptively select
samples from these two quadrants to conduct the
following steering.
3.4
Self-Explanation Guided Steering
3.4.1
Disentangled Steering
Unlike ITI (Li et al., 2023b) and CAA (Rimsky
et al., 2024), which control a single fact or style di-
rection using explicit contrastive pairs and yield
one SV, we decompose SVs into KVs and IVs
across the above quadrants to disentangle fact and
style in explainable fact-checking. Specifically, we
amplify style-sensitive signals to directly align ex-
planations with verdict via steering the model to
positive IVs’ direction. Conversely, we suppress



===== PAGE 5 =====
fact-sensitive signals to maintain consistent factual
representations via steering the model to negative
KVs’ direction. Optimal KVs or IVs are selected
by maximizing the probability gap between un-
steered and steered outputs, which aligns with ver-
dict accuracy (details in Appendix D).
Additionally, since entangled or even inconsis-
tent facts and styles weaken contrastive signals,
we adopt label-free interventions that avoid spec-
ifying only on factual directions, enabling robust
in-the-wild steering. For conceptual separability
and overhead efficiency, we use logistic probes and
intervene only at decoder blocks. The implementa-
tion details are shown in Appendix C.
3.4.2
Explanation Refinement
To further align explanation to verdict, we quantify
the alignment between each token’s hidden repre-
sentation and the optimal direction utilizing cosine
similarity. For layer l and token t, let h denote the
hidden state and s the optimal vector, the alignment
score is defined as:
al,t =
hl,t · sl
∥hl,t∥∥sl∥.
Positive alignment scores indicate alignment
with the optimal direction, while negative ones
are opposite. Segments with high-density negative
scores are often associated with redundant or noisy
style. Accordingly, we apply the lightweight Rat-
cliff–Obershelp pattern-matching algorithm (Rat-
cliff and Metzener, 1988) to suppress them and
improve explanation readability.
4
Experiments and Results
Datasets.
To better reflect real-world fact-
checking, we adopt three datasets whose claims
come from professional fact-checking platforms
and all explanations are human-written: RAW-FC
(Yang et al., 2022), Liar-RAW (Yang et al., 2022),
and AVeriTeC (Schlichtkrull et al., 2023). In RAW-
FC and LIAR-RAW, explanations directly justify
the claim label, whereas in AVeriTeC, they justify
both the claim and its supporting evidence. For
consistency, we refer to all of them as explanations
(details in Appendix E).
Metrics.
For verdict evaluation, we report Pre-
cision, Recall, and Macro-F1. For explanation
quality, both automatic and manual evaluations
are adopted: (1) Automatic Evaluations: follow-
ing (Wang et al., 2024), we employ ChatGPT as
a judge (Gu et al., 2025) across four dimensions:
Misleadingness, Informativeness, Soundness, and
Readability. Misleadingness assesses whether the
explanation is consistent with the veracity label,
and Soundness evaluates whether the explanation
seems valid and logical, which respectively re-
flect context and logical consistency on faithful-
ness hallucination (other dimensions are shown in
Appendix F). (2) Manual Evaluations: For base-
lines, we follow prior work (Wang et al., 2024)
and conduct point-wise evaluations on a subset of
RAW-FC. For ablation studies, three rounds of pair-
wise evaluations on all samples in misleadingness
are performed in Section 5.3.
4.1
Baseline Trials
4.1.1
Baselines
We incorporated the following two types of ap-
proaches as baselines: (1) External Dependency
Design: ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023), HiSS (Zhang
and Gao, 2023), FactLLaMA (Cheung and Lam,
2023), L-Defense (Wang et al., 2024), and
RAV (Shukla et al., 2025); (2) Internal Control
Design: RELFEX (ours). Among them, FactL-
LaMA also utilizes Google API to retrieve evidence
and fine-tune on LLaMA-2-7B. To ensure compara-
bility with most baselines, we adopt LLaMA2-7B
as the backbone in this section.
4.1.2
Results
For verdict prediction, Table 1 shows that our
model achieves state-of-the-art performance on
RAW-FC without relying on any closed-source
APIs, retrieved evidence, and large-scale multi-
agent system. It is API-free, evidence-free, and
agent-free. After Stage 1 (SFT), it surpasses Chat-
GPT by 16.16% F1, and outperforms HiSS by
6.69% F1. Compared with FactLLaMA, which
shares same backbone but lacks dialogue-style
modeling and full-parameter tuning, our model
achieves a 4.94% F1 gain. It performs compa-
rably to multi-agent RAV system, while using a
single model, and falls slightly below L-Defense.
After applying S-EGS, our model exceeds RAV by
5.8% F1 and outperforms L-Defense by 3.79% F1,
despite using only 465 self-extracted samples (vs.
L-Defense’s 32,240 GPT-3.5-based distillations),
highlighting remarkable data efficiency. As our
models are jointly trained with the three-way label
scheme following explanation-based method, no
comparison is made on LIAR-RAW here.

Ablation Studies
To demonstrate the generalizability, transferability,
and flexibility of REFLEX, we conduct the follow-
ing experiments on top of SFT models as baselines.
4.2.1
On Backbone Models
To explore the generalization of REFLEX, besides
LLaMA-2 7B, we also trained on a stronger back-
bone, Qwen-3 8B (Yang et al., 2025).
Overall, as shown in Table 3, S-EGS improves
performance over SFT across three datasets by up
to 5.03% in most cases, demonstrating the general-
ization ability. The two marginal drops (total drops
> 0.1) are mainly due to the severe recency bias in
few-shot learning (see Appendix J), despite several
mitigation methods addressing it (Lu et al., 2022;
Min et al., 2022; Nguyen and Wong, 2023). We
further observe that: (1) For the backbone models,
LLaMA-2 benefits more from S-EGS than Qwen-
3. (2) Among the datasets, AVeriTeC shows the
smallest gains from S-EGS, the largest improve-
ments, and the lowest hallucination rate at the SFT
stage (Appendix K). We attribute this saturation
phenomenon to its QA-style dialogue formulation,
which induces stronger knowledge generalization
and is already well aligned with our methodology
(mentioned in Section 3.2.1).

(1) Explanation as Target performs better. At
SFT stage, using explanations as targets consis-
tently improves performance.

factual knowledge from Wikipedia during pre-

4.2.2
On Cross-model Transfer
Although LLMs often overfit across domains, and
prior works (e.g., LIMA (Zhou et al., 2023) and
Character-LLM (Shao et al., 2023)) even promote
deliberate overfitting for generalization, we instead
avoid overfitting and ensure real-world applicabil-
ity in scientific studies. We conduct cross-model
steering using optimal vectors trained on Liar-RAW
and RAW-FC, excluding AVeriTeC due to its dia-
logue format differing from others, and removing
overlapping few-shot samples to prevent leakage.
As shown in Figure 4, we observe two key find-
ings. (1) The Pearson correlation between source
Macro-F and ∆target Macro-F reaches 0.95, sug-
gesting that transfer performance largely depends
on source model capacity. Strong vectors extracted
from a stronger model (source F = 64.99) substan-
tially improve weaker models (source F = 43.06)
by up to +7.54, whereas weak vectors extracted
from a weaker model (source F = 47.13) degrade
stronger models (source F = 59.39) by up to -17.31,
demonstrating performance-adaptive transfer-
ability. (2) Compared with LLaMA-2, Qwen-3
exhibits limited transferability, which is insufficient
to yield high performance gains.

5.2
Disentanglement Effectiveness
To demonstrate the effectiveness of disentangle-
ment, we conduct analyses along two axes: empiri-
cal validations and representation structures.
Empirical Validations. For empirical valida-
tions, we aggregate results from point-wise evalua-
tions, excluding readability due to post-processing,
grouped by optimal KV or IV, and further split into
correct and error cases. We find that: (1) Over-
all, KVs significantly reduce misleadingness asso-
ciated with factual labels, whereas IVs improve
reasoning quality, making it more logical and in-
formative, consistent with stylistic enhancements.
(2) Notably, under KVs, the misleadingness gap
between correct and erroneous cases is substantial
(1.52 vs. 1.74), confirming knowledge sensitivity.
These findings suggest that REFLEX successfully
disentangles IV-driven reasoning style from KV-
driven fact sensitivity.
Representation Structures. For representation
structures, we extracted each layer’s optimal KV
and IV for explanation-related pairs and computed
their direction similarity. Since each dataset ex-
hibits consistent patterns (see Appendix N), we
aggregate them and report their mean.

6
Conclusion
In this paper, we propose a straightforward yet
effective paradigm based on model internal ac-
tivations better than traditional external depen-
dency methods in fact-checking. Practically, the
self-refining approach can transfer across models,
thereby reducing training and response time. The-
oretically, it mitigates misinformation and hallu-
cination in reasoning and leads to more faithful
explanations towards veracity. Further experiments
demonstrate the generalizability and flexibility of
this paradigm across various scenarios.
Limitations
Experiment Scale.
Although we conducted
extensive ablations across backbone models,
cross-model transfer, pair combinations, and
so on, disk quota constraints and experiment
extensiveness limited it to LLaMA-2, Qwen-3, and
Mistral-v0.1 (Appendix P Table 20). Moreover,
research on real-life truth does not follow typical
scaling laws (Li et al., 2023b). Hence, we only
evaluated models at the 7 or 8B parameter scale.



===== PAGE 10 =====
Label Scheme.
While our method improves
both verdict accuracy and explanation quality,
enhancing their alignment, we adopt a 3-way label
scheme on Liar-RAW, following prior work (Wang
et al., 2024). This reflects the difficulty for LLMs
to provide explanations for overly fine-grained
label, e.g., "pants-on-fire." Importantly, our goal is
not merely to achieve state-of-the-art, but to gain
interpretable insights.
Internal Design.
Our internal interpretability-
based paradigm reduces misinformation and
response latency compared to external design
methods.
Although internal knowledge may
become outdated, this issue can be addressed
through lightweight activation editing we build
on (Section 2.3), enabling rapid incorporation
of emerging fake news.
However, existing
time-shifted datasets such as VitaminC (Schuster
et al., 2021) lack explanations, preventing direct
adaptation of our explainable paradigm.
Ethics Statement
Although our experiments are conducted on open-
sourced datasets and models, the open-sourced
models may still contain inherent social biases due
to their pretraining data. However, systematically
analyzing or mitigating social bias is beyond the
scope of this work. Our primary focus is on improv-
ing faithful hallucination and explanation quality.

Concept Alignment
Hallucination typically refers to Hallucination typ-
ically refers to the generation of content that is
either nonsensical or unfaithful to the provided
source information (Filippova, 2020; Maynez et al.,
2020).
It is application-dependent. In some domains,
such as personalized role-play dialogue (Kong
et al., 2025), creative generation (He et al., 2025),
it can be beneficial. However, in high-stakes so-
cietal domains such as law and fact-checking, or
knowledge-intensive tasks (Sun et al., 2025, 2026),
hallucinations are harmful.
In the era of large language models (LLMs), re-
cent work (Huang et al., 2025a) further categorizes
hallucinations into two main types: factuality hal-
lucinations and faithfulness hallucinations.
Factuality hallucinations arise when LLMs pro-
duce outputs that are inconsistent with real-world
facts or potentially misleading. These factual errors
are termed factuality hallucinations.
Faithfulness hallucinations concern whether
the generated content remains consistent with
user instructions, provided context, and inter-
nal logical coherence.
Accordingly, they can
be categorized into instruction inconsistency,
context inconsistency, and logical inconsistency.
We argue that, in essence, this type of halluci-
nation can be understood as context inconsistency.
Misinformation
refers
to
false
or
inaccu-
rate information that is deliberately created and is
either intentionally or unintentionally propagated.
In the context of social media, (Chen and Shu,
2024) adopts a broader view, treating misinforma-
tion as an umbrella term that encompasses all false
or inaccurate information circulating on social
media platforms.
Under this definition, misinformation can be cat-
egorized into several types, including fake news,
rumors, conspiracy theories, clickbait, misleading
claims, and cherry-picking.
Moreover, they suggest that LLM-generated
misinformation may exhibit more deceptive
styles, thereby posing a greater potential risk of
real-world harm.
Misinformation vs.
Hallucination.
(Chen
and Shu, 2024) shows that hallucination is one
of the sources of misinformation, while (Huang
et al., 2025a) indicates that misinformation is one
of the causes of hallucination. Therefore, they
can be regarded as twins — mutually influencing
each other and forming a bidirectional causal
relationship.
Knowledge vs.
Style.
“IVs extracted from
Q2 samples capture style-dependent veracity
gains” is supported by prior empirical works:
- OpenAI’s fine-tuning guide notes that, fine-
tuning performs well in show-not-tell scenario:
“show” corresponds to stylistic patterns, whereas
“tell” corresponds to knowledge injection.
- Several studies (Ren et al., 2024; Ghosal et al.,
2024; Zhao et al., 2025) argue that injecting spe-
cific or new knowledge via fine-tuning is compara-
tively difficult. (Zhao et al., 2025) finds that fine-
tuning on the latest Wikipedia events (knowledge)
performs worse, whereas fine-tuning on writing
with a specific tone (style) yields near-perfect be-
havior.
- (Yan et al., 2025) prove that probability-based
LLMs inference is actually the memorized stylistic
pattern in reasoning task.
“KVs extracted from Q4 samples isolate fact-
sensitive veracity drops” is similarly supported by
prior empirical findings.
- (Li et al., 2023c) pointed out backbone models
have encoded extensive factual knowledge (section
3.2.1 lines 203-207).
- (Gekhman et al., 2024) reports that fine-tuning
on new knowledge can increase hallucination risk,
especially in dynamic knowledge in fact-checking.
- In alignment research, this phenomenon is
sometimes informally referred to as an alignment
tax (Leike, 2022).
Transferred to our task, this literature suggests a
natural mapping: style corresponds to reasoning
style, while knowledge corresponds to factual
grounding. This distinction motivates separating
IV-driven style from KV-driven fact sensitivity.
Faithfulness vs.
Plausibility on NLE. Nat-
ural Language Explanations (NLEs) refer to
explanations
expressed
in
natural
language.
According to (Agarwal et al., 2024), recent
works show that modern LLMs can generate
self-explanations (SEs),
which aim to elicit
intermediate reasoning steps to account for their
outputs. They define the concepts of faithfulness
and plausibility.



===== PAGE 15 =====
- Plausibility concerns whether an explanation
aligns with human reasoning and understanding. In
this work, we treat it as a stylistic pattern.
- Faithfulness evaluates whether an explanation
accurately reflects the model’s underlying reason-
ing process. However, since we do not assume that
LLMs possess genuine reasoning capabilities, our
notion of faithfulness is framed through the lens of
faithfulness hallucinations.

Dataset Details
We use three fact-checking datasets: RAW-FC from
Snopes2, LIAR-RAW (Yang et al., 2022) from Poli-
tiFact3, and AVeriTeC. For RAW-FC and LIAR-
RAW, evidence corresponds to the annotated rel-
evant evidence (labeled as 1). Since AVeriTeC is
natively multi-turn, for consistency, we flatten each
dialogue into a single-turn instance.
In terms of data structure, RAW-FC and LIAR-
RAW follow the standard fact-checking format
claim, evidence, label, explanation. In contrast,
AVeriTeC decomposes fact-checking into a QA-
style, multi-turn verification process.
The datasets adopt different label schemes.
RAW-FC uses True, Half-True, False, LIAR-RAW
contains six labels, and AVeriTeC uses Supported,
Not Enough Evidence, Conflicting Evidence/Cher-
rypicking, Refuted. To support joint verdict predic-
2www.snopes.com
3www.politifact.com
tion and explanation generation in REFLEX, we
unify labels as follows. For LIAR-RAW, we merge
pants-fire, false, barely-true into False, retain Half-
True, and merge mostly-true, true into True. For
AVeriTeC, we discard Conflicting Evidence/Cher-
rypicking due to its ambiguity. We further remove
LIAR-RAW instances without evidence and ex-
clude AVeriTeC few-shot examples from validation
to prevent data leakage.
As shown in Table 8, the training sample sizes
for three datasets are no more than 10K, which
is suitable for our paradigm’s assumption - fine-
tuning on limited data. Among them, RAW-FC is
label-balanced, whereas LIAR-RAW and AVeriTeC
are not. Since AVeriTeC does not release a test set
to avoid leakage, we use its validation set for evalu-
ation after removing training overlaps. Finally, be-
cause some baselines cannot handle dialogue-style
inputs, baseline comparisons are reported only on
RAW-FC and LIAR-RAW.
Since the human-annotated RAW-FC test set
seems small, similar to other popular benchmarks
such as MT-Bench built manually, we addition-
ally computed confidence intervals using bootstrap
resampling on ablation studies - section one (see
Appendix L).

Full Baselines Comparsions
Due to space constraints in main text, we defer the
full analysis of baselines and time complexity here.
As shown in Table 9, distill/RAG-then-SFT
paradigms (e.g., FACTLLAMA and L-DEFENSE)
incur the highest time cost, as they require col-
lecting multiple pieces of evidence per claim from
closed-source models or search APIs, followed by
task-specific fine-tuning. For m claims and n as-
sociated evidence, both scale as O(mn), with L-
DEFENSE further introducing adversarial explana-
tions during distillation. Multi-agent approaches
such as RAV remove training overhead but intro-
duce substantial inference-time cost: each claim in-
volves Z agents to deploy, (Z−1) interactions, and
Z inference steps, resulting in O(mZ) complex-
ity. RAG-based methods (e.g., HISS) decompose
each claim into n sub-claims and iteratively per-
form retrieval, requiring repeated interactions with
external APIs and yielding O(mn) complexity.
In contrast, REFLEX eliminates reliance on
closed-source APIs, external evidence, and multi-
agent systems (C = 0, O = 0, Z = 0). Although
it needs time to train and extract vectors, once opti-
mal vectors are extracted, they can be transferred
to other models with only a constant number of
inference steps per claim, resulting in linear time
complexity O(m).
Notably, here we introduce DeReC (Qazi et al.,
2025), a concurrent state-of-the-art method for fact-
checking. It is a retrieval-augmented fact verifica-
tion framework that combines dense embedding-
based evidence retrieval with FAISS-based similar-
ity search and a DeBERTa-v3 classifier to predict
claim veracity. However, despite its efficiency and
strong performance among claim verification sys-
tems, it still underperforms REFLEX in terms of
Macro-F1. Also, following (Wang et al., 2024),
we exclude a high-potential model JustiLM (Zeng
and Gao, 2024) that leverages external evidence, as
its proposed explanations (justifications) are jointly
conditioned on both the claim and the evidence. In
our baseline setting, explanations in both datasets
pertain solely to the claim itself, which is unfair for
this powerful model.

All of the annotators are undergraduate students

a point-wise evaluation for explanations on each di-

test instances from RAW-FC using a 5-point Likert
scale. Model identities were kept anonymous, and
the average scores were used as the final metric.
Table 13 shows that all four dimensions have cor-

Our S-EGS outperforms other baselines in all di-
mensions. For inter-annotator agreement, since
these are point-wise evaluations, we also computed
Cronbach’s α. The overall average α is 0.7896,
which is acceptable according to convention.
For ablative studies, we conducted a pair-wise
evaluation for explanations of our focus misleading-
ness dimensions. Three undergraduate annotators
rated each sample on the testing set from RAW-
FC, LIAR-RAW, and AVeriTeC. Each annotator
was instructed to compare the explanations of two
models and determine which one exhibited more
misleadingness. The model names remained anony-
mous, and the positions of the model outputs were
randomly swapped. For inter-annotator agreement,
as these are pairwise evaluations, we further com-
puted Fleiss’ Kappa. The overall average Kappa is
0.6963, indicating substantial agreement.

generate explanations for double validation.

disentangled vectors across two backbone models.
Figure 9 shows that: for Dual w/o exp. pairs, the

whereas for pairs with full explanations, diver-
gences peak in the middle layers (10–20). This
aligns with prior findings on transformer-based
model interpretability: early layers capture lexical
and topical signals, middle layers encode stylistic
and syntactic patterns (Yun et al., 2021), and higher
layers capture conceptual information (Yun et al.,
2021). Interestingly, this middle-layer dominance
exists across subjective styles (e.g., sycophancy

from common misconceptions (Li et al., 2023b) to
fine-grained fact-checking misinformation. This
consistent pattern lets us rethink that contrastive
signals mainly capture surface-level stylistic pat-
terns of reasoning or knowledge representations.

are dominated by noisy, redundant syntax patterns.
This confirms REFLEX successfully disentangles
the surface-level veracity and explanation styles.

More Analysis of Statistical
Correlations
As shown in Figure 6, three findings emerge: (1)
Performance is primarily driven by faithfulness-
related metrics: both F-score and accuracy are
strongly negatively correlated with misleadingness
(-0.85, -0.96) and strongly positively correlated
with soundness (0.91, 0.78). (2) Readability plays
a secondary but consistent role: it correlates posi-
tively with both F-score (0.67) and accuracy(0.81),
suggesting that clearer explanations tend to align
with better predictions. (3) Informativeness ex-
hibits a clear trade-off with effectiveness. It is
negatively correlated with readability (-0.35) and
accuracy (-0.04), while positively correlated with
both misleadingness (0.26) and soundness (0.57),
indicating that adding more background informa-
tion can make explanations appear more logically
grounded, but often introduces extra noise that re-
duces clarity and hurts decision quality.
