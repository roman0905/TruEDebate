Abstract
Recent studies show that claims incorporating both text and images
spread more effectively than those with text alone, presenting signif-
icant challenges for multimodal fact-checking. The rapid develop-
ment of Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) has greatly
advanced research in this field, enabling stronger performance.
However, existing MLLM-based fact-checking methods fail to fully
exploit visual evidence, and their reliance on rigid fine-tuning tem-
plates limits context-aware explanations and leads to weak deep rea-
soning. To address these limitations, we propose FACTCOMPASS, a
novel framework that combines reasoning-aware fine-tuning with
large-scale rule-based reinforcement learning and incorporates a
semantic- and knowledge-enhanced retrieval module to strengthen
deep reasoning and improve evidence utilization. This framework
enhances evidence retrieval by obtaining semantically relevant
evidence images, enriching the contextual understanding of claim-
related images, and refining textual evidence at the knowledge level.
To further enhance reasoning, we introduce a self-refining reinforce-
ment fine-tuning strategy: (1) distilling GPT-4o’s reasoning from
partially fact-checking data for cold-start Chain-of-Thought learn-
ing; (2) activating reasoning across broader datasets using prior

ensuring information credibility. As social media and generative AI
accelerate the dissemination of information at an unprecedented

Information is conveyed through multimodal media, primarily
text and images. Existing text-based fact-checking methods have
achieved significant progress. However, with the rise of multime-
dia technologies, an increasing number of rumor spreaders are
leveraging combinations of text and images to attract attention and
spread multimodal false information [35, 44]. Common types of
multimodal misinformation can be categorized into two groups:
one involves fabricated text or images [23, 34], and the other [1, 39]
involves authentic images presented in new but misleading or incor-
rect contexts—a phenomenon often referred to as “out-of-context”.
Unlike unimodal text-based fact-checking, text and images are
inherently heterogeneous, making it challenging to detect false
information from such entangled multimodal content.
Early methods [6, 12, 49] fuse multimodal features from claims
and evidence using multimodal encoders, followed by a fact-checking
classifier to make predictions (as illustrated in Figure 1(a)). These
approaches typically adopt concatenation operations or attention
mechanisms to capture the underlying semantic relationships be-
tween the claims. However, they often struggle to fully comprehend
the semantics of news content. In addition, due to inherent model
limitations, such methods generally fail to generate convincing and
interpretable fact-checking outcomes. In recent years, multimodal
large language models (MLLMs) have made significant strides in
integrating visual and textual information. Building upon this, sev-
eral studies [29] have fine-tuned large language models specifically
for fact-checking tasks (as shown in Figure 1(b)), where multimodal
claims and evidence are organized into supervised fine-tuning (SFT)
datasets to train open-source MLLMs. Nevertheless, these methods
still face several key challenges: (1) they fail to fully utilize im-
age evidence, overlooking the learning and fine-grained alignment
of visual cues; (2) they rely on rigid processing pipelines that are
error-prone and lack robust deep reasoning capabilities, ultimately
constraining the model’s ability to understand complex and realistic
news contexts.
To address the limitations of existing methods in image utiliza-
tion and reasoning capabilities, we propose FACTCOMPASS, a
novel framework that combines multi-level retrieval modules with
a self-optimizing reinforcement fine-tuning strategy to advance
the development of multimodal fact-checking. The framework is
specifically designed to overcome two core challenges in current ap-
proaches: (1) To address the insufficient use of image evidence and
the lack of fine-grained visual alignment, FACTCOMPASS intro-
duces a Semantic-enhanced Retrieval Module, which constructs a
dedicated claim-evidence image pair dataset and adopts a Fact NCE
loss function to learn a unified feature space between claims and
evidence images. This enables accurate matching of semantically
relevant image evidence, thereby enhancing the model’s ability
to understand and reason over visual information. Additionally,
a Knowledge-enhanced Retrieval Module is well-designed to in-
corporate knowledge graphs and structured knowledge bases (e.g.,
Wikidata) via SearchAgent and RefineAgent, supplementing and op-
timizing textual evidence to enhance contextual understanding and
retrieval efficiency. (2) To tackle rigid processing pipelines and weak
reasoning capabilities, FACTCOMPASS proposes a reinforcement
learning method named Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)
to replace traditional template-based reasoning. This approach en-
courages the model to explore diverse and deeper reasoning paths
through trial-and-error.
The overall multimodal fact-checking process of FACTCOM-
PASS is illustrated in Figure 1(c), consisting of three major com-
ponents: (1) Semantic-enhanced Retrieval Module: Enhances se-
mantic matching of image evidence through multi-source image
retrieval and alignment in a unified feature space. (2) Knowledge-
enhanced Retrieval Module: Expands the context of textual evidence
using named entity recognition and knowledge graph linking. The
SearchAgent retrieves relevant knowledge, while the RefineAgent
refines the results to improve information quality. (3) Large Lan-
guage Model Training Pipeline: Composed of three stages: 1) Cold-
Start: Establishes a reasoning foundation by leveraging GPT-4o to
generate initial reasoning data. 2) Rejection Sampling: Generates
and filters long-chain reasoning trajectories to reinforce accurate
patterns. 3) GRPO: Uses reinforcement learning to optimize rea-
soning strategies, promoting active reasoning evolution beyond
traditional supervised fine-tuning. Through the synergy of these
modules, FACTCOMPASS forms a closed-loop system in image
understanding, textual refinement, and reasoning evolution, effec-
tively improving the accuracy, flexibility, and interpretability of
multimodal fact-checking.

FACTCOMPASS, a novel multimodal fact-checking framework that
integrates multi-level retrieval modules and a self-refining reinforce-
ment fine-tuning strategy to jointly improve image evidence utiliza-
tion and reasoning capabilities. (2) We design a semantic-enhanced
retrieval module with a dedicated claim-image dataset and a Fact
NCE loss to learn a unified feature space, enabling fine-grained
alignment between claims and image evidence. (3) We introduce

Related Work
2.1
Multi-modal Fact-checking.
Prior research on multimodal fact-checking has primarily focused
on textual analysis, with limited utilization of visual information.
Early approaches, such as Jaiswal et al. [17], matched random image-
title pairs, while Sabir et al. [31] advanced this by swapping named
entities to generate more challenging mismatches. Khattar et al. [18]
introduced the Multimodal Variational Autoencoder (MVAE), which
combines a bimodal autoencoder with a classifier to learn shared
text-visual representations. However, many fabricated examples in
early datasets were overly simplistic or linguistically biased, mak-
ing them easily detectable by basic models [22]. To address these
limitations, Luo et al. [22] developed the NewsCLIPpings dataset,
while datasets such as Factify [28] further enriched multimodal
misinformation detection with more diverse and realistic samples.
Recent advancements have emphasized cross-modal consistency
and reasoning. Sahar et al. [1] introduced a consistency-checking
network (CCN) to align visual and textual modalities. Dhankar et
al. [10] applied cosine similarity between text and visual embed-
dings, while Du et al. [11] fused DeBERTa and Swinv2 embeddings
using co-attention. More recent contributions include Hu et al. [15],
who constructed the 𝑀𝑅2 dataset for multimodal reasoning, and
the creation of MOCHEG [45], a large-scale fact-checking dataset
containing 21,184 annotated claims for training robust multimodal
models. AVeriTeC [32] further advances this direction by providing
real-world multimodal evidence retrieved from the web for claim
verification. DEFAME [5] adopts a six-stage inference-time RAG
pipeline with a frozen MLLM, demonstrating strong generalization
and factual reasoning capabilities.
2.2
Multi-modal Large language Models.
Recent Multi-modal Large language Models have made remarkable
progress in visual perception and cross-modal understanding. BLIP-
2 [19] pioneered image-text feature fusion via LLM integration,
while LLaVA [21] and Mini-GPT [51] further advanced contextual
alignment through instruction tuning. Models such as QwenVL
[3], InternVL [7], and YiVL [46] enhanced multimodal reasoning
through large-scale pre-training and adaptive fusion mechanisms.
Meanwhile, Gemini [37] and SNIFFER [29] demonstrated strong
capabilities in misinformation detection and fact verification tasks.
With the emergence of advanced LLMs like OpenAI o1 [16] and
DeepSeek R1 [13], MLLMs are increasingly capable of handling
complex visual reasoning, leveraging methods such as visual chain-
of-thought reasoning [33, 40] and retrieval-augmented reasoning
[42] to improve interpretability and factual consistency.

Methodology
3.1
Architecture Overview
The architecture of FACTCOMPASS, shown in Figure 2 and Figure 3,
comprises three core and interrelated components: the Semantic-
enhanced Retrieval Module, the Knowledge-enhanced Retrieval
Module, and the LLM module. To further enhance reasoning ca-
pability and overall robustness, we introduce a three-stage self-
refining reinforcement fine-tuning approach to effectively train the
LLM module.
3.2
Semantic-enhanced Retrieval Module
Since the original CLIP architecture was pre-trained on natural
image datasets, it lacks support for semantic alignment within
the news domain and for dual image-to-image and image-to-text
matching. Our goal is to develop a specialized claim-evidence image
retrieval module that selects evidence images (along with their
corresponding captions) most semantically aligned with the claim
image. This approach enables precise matching and improves the
model’s ability to understand news facts for fact-checking. During
training, we retain the original news captions for each evidence
image in the retrieval set, sourced from online documents, to ensure
semantic and contextual consistency.
Module Detail. Given CLIP’s outstanding performance in zero-
shot perception, which enables effective matching and distinction
of general-domain natural images, we built our retrieval module
based on the CLIP model, as illustrated in Figure 2. In this setup,
CLIP’s image and text encoders are frozen to preserve their pre-
trained knowledge. Building on this foundation, we introduce two
MLP layers adapter structures after the encoders to fully leverage
CLIP’s potential in the domain of image matching for news content.

3.3
Knowledge-enhanced Retrieval Module
To enhance textual evidence, FACTCOMPASS introduces a Knowledge-
enhanced Retrieval Module, which consists of the SearchAgent for
external knowledge retrieval and the RefineAgent for knowledge
refinement within documents.
SearchAgent. Claim images often encode rich background knowl-
edge that supports fact-checking by providing context and improv-
ing interpretability. We first extract visual entities 𝐸𝑖—such as peo-
ple and locations—using the Google Entity Detection API and YOLO.
These entities are then enriched through two stages: first, they are
linked to the DBpedia knowledge graph to retrieve higher-order se-
mantic information, including one-hop neighboring entities, which
are converted into token sequences using a pre-trained model;
second, we query Wikidata knowledge base to retrieve concise,
entity-specific descriptions, which are contextualized into complete
background sentences to support claim understanding in the news
domain.

and select the preferred reasoning via manual diversity evaluation.
This process focuses the training distribution on accurate reason-
ing patterns and enhances the model’s generalization ability across
different visual domains.

Dataset. Due to the limitations of available training data, we for-

VisualNews corpus [20], where both the images and captions remain
unaltered, though some are mismatched. We follow the standard
protocol by using the provided balanced subset with corresponding
evidence, treating the task as binary classification and reporting
accuracy (Acc) as the evaluation metric. Evidence retrieval and
augmentation follow the method described in [1]. (2) OOC-MFC,

standard deviation. (4) Finally, we evaluate our model on the CMFC

comprising 46K claims. It includes two subsets: CCMF and SCMF.

Implementation Details. All core experiments were conducted
three times, and the mean values are reported. We adopt Qwen2.5-
VL as the base MLLM, and the training process consists of three
stages. DBpedia [2] serves as the background knowledge graph.
All experiments are implemented in PyTorch and executed on four
NVIDIA Tesla H100 GPUs. In the Semantic-enhanced Retrieval Mod-
ule, the number of evidence images is set to 3; in the SearchAgent, the
pre-trained model for converting graph triples into token sequences
is gpt4o-mini; and in the RefineAgent, the similarity threshold is 0.7.
In Stage 1 (Cold Start), we initialize training with 20K high-quality
GPT-4o-generated samples from the NewsCLIPpings dataset, evenly
split between real and fake claims. The visual encoder is frozen,
and LoRA is applied for efficient fine-tuning with lora_r = 128,
lora_alpha = 256, and lr = 2e-5; the multimodal projector uses
a learning rate of 2e-6. Training runs for 2 epochs with a batch
size of 2 using AdamW and cosine annealing scheduling. In Stage 2
(Rejection Sampling), we retain model-generated responses with
accurate reasoning and manually select the best explanations from
three temperature-varied outputs (0–1), yielding 20K refined sam-
ples. LoRA is reused with lora_r = 64, lora_alpha = 128, and
lr = 2e-5, while keeping the projector learning rate at 2e-6 and
the visual encoder frozen. In Stage 3 (GRPO), Group-based Relative
Policy Optimization is applied with a verifiability-based reward.
The model samples eight candidate outputs per input and is trained
for 2 epochs on 20K news samples with lr = 2e-5. Final results
are reported as the average over three independent runs to ensure
stability and reliability.

4.3
Explainability Analysis
To further assess effectiveness in revealing misinformation, we se-
lected a subset of 100 challenging multimodal claims that all five
models in Figure 4 failed to classify correctly—comprising 50 real
claims incorrectly predicted as fake, and 50 fake claims incorrectly
predicted as real, resulting in 0% accuracy across the board. For
each instance, we retained the model-generated explanation. To
simulate human fact-checking, we designed a prompt that inputs
each claim and its explanation into GPT-4o, asking it to make a
secondary judgment (real or fake). As shown in Figure 4, expla-
nations generated by FACTCOMPASS significantly improved the
secondary review accuracy, while GPT-4o’s own explanations of-
ten amplified hallucinations and biases. Additional analysis can be
found in Supplementary Materials.
4.4
Ablation Studies
We conduct ablation studies to evaluate the impact of each mod-
ule in FACTCOMPASS, as shown in Table 3. (1) For the Semantic-
enhanced Retrieval Module, removing either the evidence image
token (w/o Evidence Image) or the retrieval module (w/o Retrieval,
replaced with CLIP similarity) leads to notable performance drops,
highlighting the module’s importance for aligning visual evidence.
(2) For the Knowledge-enhanced Retrieval Module, omitting the
RefineAgent degrades performance, confirming its role in extract-
ing concise, relevant information from textual evidence. Removing
either the knowledge graph or knowledge base from the SearchA-
gent also reduces accuracy, demonstrating the benefit of multi-level
external knowledge. (3) Finally, ablating components of the self-
refining reinforcement fine-tuning strategy—w/o Cold Start, w/o
Reject, and w/o GRPO—shows that each stage plays a critical role in
gradually enhancing the model’s reasoning ability.
4.5
Qualitative Evaluation
Figure 5 presents four examples that were correctly identified, with
enhanced evidence text and evidence image provided by the two
retrieval modules. By examining these examples, we observe that
our semantic-enhanced retrieval module can effectively uncover
evidence images semantically similar to the claim image and re-
fine the associated evidence text. Furthermore, the SearchAgent
effectively captures key knowledge embedded in the claim image,
providing valuable background information for the news content.
For instance, in example (c), the news content is relatively obscure,
but by retrieving the entity ‘Hong Kong’ and its contextual de-
scription, the model fully comprehends the news, aiding in fake
claim detection. In example (d), By converting news background
knowledge into textual modality, the model improves its under-
standing of world locations like ‘Glasgow’ in relation to the claim
image, thereby enhancing the multimodal fact-checking process.
Furthermore, our model not only produces accurate predictions but
also generates coherent and interpretable reasoning chains.
5
Conclusion
We propose FACTCOMPASS, a comprehensive and unified multi-
modal fact-checking framework designed to enhance both reason-
ing depth and retrieval precision through three synergistic com-
ponents: semantic-enhanced evidence image retrieval, knowledge-
enriched textual refinement, and a self-refining reinforcement fine-
tuning strategy. By jointly optimizing semantic understanding,
knowledge grounding, and reasoning adaptability, FACTCOMPASS
achieves more accurate and interpretable multimodal fact verifi-
cation. Extensive experiments further validate its superior per-
formance, robust reasoning ability, and high interpretability in
misinformation detection and related applications.

Consider the following when making your assessment:
•Consistency: Does the claim text align with the content of the image? Are there any contradictions

•Relevance of Explainability: Does the explainability provide additional context or clarification

•Contextual Accuracy: Are there any factual inaccuracies in the claim text, image, or explanation?

•If the claim text and image are aligned and supported by the explainability, the claim is "real".
