Abstract
Existing challenges in misinformation exposure
and susceptibility vary across demographics, as
some populations are more vulnerable to mis-
information than others. Large language mod-
els (LLMs) introduce new dimensions to these
challenges through their ability to generate per-
suasive content at scale and reinforcing exist-
ing biases. Our study introduces PANDORA,
a framework that investigates the bidirectional
persuasion dynamics between LLMs and hu-
mans when exposed to misinformative content.
We use a multi-agent LLM framework to ana-
lyze the spread of misinformation under persua-
sion among demographic-oriented LLM agents.
Our findings show that demographic factors
influence LLM susceptibility, with up to 15
percentage point differences in misinformation
correctness across groups. Multi-agent LLMs
also exhibit echo chamber behavior, aligning
with human-like group polarization patterns.
Therefore, this work highlights demographic
divides in misinformation dynamics and offers
insights for future interventions.

Introduction
In an era of rapid information exchange, misinfor-
mation poses a significant social challenge (Broda
and Strömbäck, 2024; Sultan et al., 2024) with its
impact shaped by diverse backgrounds, belief sys-
tems, and access to information (Verma et al., 2022;
Knuutila et al., 2022; Chandrasekaran et al., 2024).
Furthermore, the increasing use of LLMs to
search for information, ask questions, or make
judgments about the credibility of the content,
brings significant opportunities and risks in this
landscape (Garry et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b,
2025). While previous research has highlighted the
capabilities of LLMs in countering misinformation
through well-designed interventions (Gabriel et al.,
2024), they can also be misused to craft persua-
sive narratives (Danry et al., 2022) and amplify the
spread of misinformation.

The paper makes the following contributions:
First, we use LLMs to generate persuasive argu-

claim. We then assess how individuals from di-

arguments and examine their susceptibility to mis-
information (Fig. 1 (1)). Second, we take human-

susceptibility to misinformation. (Fig 1 (2)). Third,
we examine the effectiveness of persuasive argu-
ments generated by humans and LLMs, as well

design a multi-agent LLM architecture to study the
effect of both human- and LLM-driven persuasion
on interactions involving demographic-aware LLM
personas (Fig. 2). This setup allows to investigate
how misinformation is perpetuated and to analyze
LLM behavior in complex multi-agent settings. By
integrating both human and LLM perspectives, our
study aims to provide insights into how persua-
sion operates in human-LLM interactions while
accounting for demographic factors.
2
Related Work
LLM-generated Persuasion. Recent research has
examined the persuasive capabilities of LLMs, in-
cluding their ability to influence others (Gabriel
et al., 2024; Matz et al., 2024) and their susceptibil-
ity to being influenced (Griffin et al., 2023; Chen
et al., 2024). Studies have shown that LLMs can
play a dual role in persuasion: they can be benefi-
cial, as shown by Gabriel et al. (2024), and poten-
tially harmful, as highlighted by Danry et al. (2022).
Understanding these dynamics is critical, as LLMs
are increasingly integrated into human-facing ap-
plications, from education and customer service to
social media and healthcare tools (Andrew, 2024;
Nair et al., 2024). Their persuasive power can
shape opinions, behaviors, and beliefs, making it es-
sential to assess how they influence human thought
processes and decision-making (Breum et al., 2024;
Salvi et al., 2024).
Human-LLM Misinformation Dynamics. Sev-
eral studies have examined the impact of LLM-
generated misinformation on humans. Chen and
Shu (2023) found that such misinformation is more
difficult to detect for both humans and automated
systems. Moreover, recent studies have shown that
LLM-generated misinformation can significantly
degrade the performance of Open-Domain Ques-
tion Answering (ODQA) systems, highlighting the
cascading effects of synthetic misinformation (Pan
et al., 2023). Furthermore, LLMs have been shown
to be vulnerable to misinformation attacks that can
alter their internal knowledge representations (Han
et al., 2024). Despite these findings, limited re-
search has explored the mutual influence of persua-
sion in humans and LLMs (Salvi et al., 2024). To
our knowledge, our study is the first to systemati-
cally compare and contrast the quality and impact
of human and LLM-driven persuasion in the con-
text of misinformation, offering novel insights into
their bidirectional interactions.
Multi-Agent LLM Simulations. LLMs have been
increasingly studied as tools to simulate social sys-
tems (Park et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024). Pre-
vious work has shown that LLM agents produce
plausible individual and emergent social behaviors,
collaborating or competing to achieve complex so-
cial goals. Although computational models have
been developed to simulate human susceptibility
to misinformation in various demographics (Liu
et al., 2024), multi-agent LLM simulations remain
underexplored in this domain. For instance, Li
et al. (2024) examined how LLM personality traits,
such as extraversion, influence the dissemination
of information within groups. However, their work
did not address demographic-oriented LLM simula-
tions, leaving open questions on how demographic
factors shape misinformation dynamics in multi-
agent LLM systems. Our study addresses this gap
by introducing, to our knowledge, the first use of
demographic-aware multi-agent LLM interactions
in the context of misinformation.

3.2
Human-to-LLM Persuasion
This component evaluates how persuasive argu-
ments written by humans influence LLMs re-
sponses. Fig. 1 (2) shows an overview of the setup,
where LLMs are shown human-authored arguments
supporting or refuting the given claim, and we as-
sess how these arguments influence their beliefs.
As before, the process consists of two stages:
(1) Human Persuasion: We use existing human-
stance datasets (details in Sec 4.1) that contain ar-
guments written by humans expressing support or
opposition towards specific claims.
(2) LLM Response Evaluation: We assess the
influence of human persuasion on demographic-
aware LLM personas. First, the LLM is instructed
to adopt a demographic persona (rural, urban, fe-
male, male, young, old) using the prompt “Assume
you belong to <group>”, next it is presented
with human-authored arguments and asked to state
their belief in the claim. Full prompt details are
illustrated in the Appendix D.2. We also perform
experiments with a no-persona LLM baseline to
disentangle intrinsic model behavior from persona-
induced effects in Appendix D.5.
3.3
Multi-agent LLM Persuasion
Misinformation affects demographic groups differ-
ently, leading to varying levels of belief and skepti-
cism toward claims (Verma et al., 2022; Knuutila
et al., 2022; Chandrasekaran et al., 2024). Indi-
viduals are more likely to trust information from
their own demographic (in-group) more than from
others (out-group), perceiving it as more credi-
ble (Ma et al., 2019; Hinsley, 2021). This ten-
dency reinforces echo chamber behavior, where
individuals primarily engage with in-group peers,
amplifying shared beliefs and filtering opposing
views (Nikolov et al., 2020).
We investigate whether similar dynamics emerge
from multi-agent LLM interactions. Building upon
our single-agent component, we explore how both
human- and LLM generated persuasive arguments
affect belief formation in multi-agent LLMs across
demographics.
Multi-agent LLM architecture: In line with our
previous components, we use the same set of demo-
graphic groups to initialize personas for LLM inter-
actions. To better understand the beliefs these per-
sonas reflect, we conduct a qualitative analysis of
persona-specific beliefs, detailed in Appendix F.2.
Our findings show that demographic LLM personas
often produce stereotypical associations - for ex-
ample, portraying men as achievement-oriented,
women as vulnerable/empathetic, rural individuals
as traditional, urban as progressive or innovation-
oriented, the young as activist and the old as conser-
vative. Although these patterns align with certain
social science constructs (Hofstede, 2011; Villa and
Moya, 2020; Kunkel and Settersten, 2021; Busso-
letti, 2023; Cheshmehzangi et al., 2024), they also
underscore the potential for stereotype reinforce-
ment. This highlights the need for caution when

The multi-agent setup involves two agents in-
teracting to explore how in-group (homogeneous)
vs. out-group (heterogeneous) dynamics affect the
acceptance or rejection of information. We choose
two-agent interactions as it is considered a standard
testbed in social psychology and social network
analysis across polarization studies (Müller et al.,
2022; Leskovec et al., 2010; Shi et al., 2016). Addi-
tionally, it provides a good trade-off between com-
putational costs and controlled trials across models
and conditions. Note that we use LLM multi-agent
research as a scalable testbed and hypothesis gen-
erator that complements existing human-group re-
search.
In the homogeneous setting, both agents share
the same demographic identity (e.g., female-
female, rural-rural), while in a heterogeneous set-
ting, agents represent different groups (e.g., female-
male, rural-urban). Fig. 2 shows an overview of
this setup. The interaction unfolds over four rounds.
In the first round, agents independently assess the
claim and make an initial judgment. In the second
round, they are shown persuasive arguments sup-
porting and refuting the claim. This is followed by
two rounds of agent-to-agent interactions, where
they attempt to persuade each other while remain-
ing open to opposing perspectives. In the final
round, agents make their final judgments, deciding
whether they believe the claim is true or false. Our
setup draws inspiration from social science studies
on group behavior (Lord, 2015; Rania et al., 2021)
and multi-agent societal simulations (Borah and
Mihalcea, 2024). (See Appendix F.1 for prompt
details).
3.4
Evaluating Persuasion
Persuasion is the extent to which a message influ-
ences a recipient’s beliefs, attitudes, or judgments
toward a target claim (Perloff, 1993). We opera-
tionalize persuasion as its measurable effect on re-
cipient’s beliefs: given a claim xi and accompany-
ing persuasive stances, we treat each response as a
binary belief label (believe/disbelieve xi) and com-
pute a correctness/accuracy rate against the ground-
truth veracity yi (e.g., CRhuman for humans and
CRLLM for LLM personas). Importantly, we do not
measure persuasion via a within-subject (with vs.
without persuasion) comparison for the same indi-
viduals, since repeated exposure can introduce car-
ryover, anchoring, and demand effects that bias be-
lief judgments (Reichardt, 2002; Orne, 2017; Tver-
sky and Kahneman, 1974; Zajonc, 1968). There-
fore, we evaluate persuasion effects using accuracy
based on the claim veracity. Finally, we comple-
ment this evaluation with linguistic analyses of
human- and LLM-persuasive texts (e.g., lexical di-
versity, readability, psychology markers, etc.).

4.2
Participant Recruitment and Survey
Design
We recruited participants via the Prolific3 platform.
We conducted surveys on three demographic di-
mensions: location (rural and urban), gender (fe-
male, male), and age (young: under 30 years; older:
over 60 years). Informed consent was obtained
from all participants prior participation. Each par-
ticipant was asked to evaluate 3 pieces of informa-
tion as shown in Fig. 3. We deliberately constrain
the per-participant load to reduce fatigue and main-
tain data quality, which can be especially useful
in a cognitively demanding task setting such as
judging the quality of information (Behrens et al.,
2023). For each item, participants indicated their
belief in the claim, whether they have encountered
the information before, and responded to a brief de-
mographic questionnaire. At the end of the survey,
participants were debriefed about the misinforma-
tive nature of the content and informed that the
stances were produced by LLMs. We recruited a
total of 302 participants, with a minimum of 95
participants per demographic category. We ensure
that every item is evaluated by at least one par-
ticipant. Therefore, our human study consists of
302 x 3 = 906 decisions, which provides sufficient
power for the main research questions. Additional
details about recruitment procedures and survey
implementation are provided in Appendix C.3.

Connecting Our Findings to Prior Research.
Several studies have examined demographic dif-
ferences in susceptibility to misinformation. Re-
garding gender differences, prior research presents
mixed findings. Some show that women are more
likely to believe and share misinformation (Pan
et al., 2021; Peter et al., 2024; Kyrychenko et al.,
2025), and despite greater concern, they do not
show higher accuracy in identifying it (Almenar
et al., 2021; Enock et al., 2024). While other stud-
ies show that women are less likely than men to
endorse conspiracy theories (Cassese et al., 2020).
In our analyses, we find that both female human
participants and LLM-based female personas show
lower correctness rates than their male counterparts,
aligning with some of the studies above.
Taking into account rural/urban differences, the
findings of our study also align with patterns of
previous research showing that rural communities
are more vulnerable to misinformation Lister and
Joudrey (2022), often due to lack of access to credi-
ble and comprehensive news media, creating “news
deserts” (Lee and Bissell, 2022).
Finally, in terms of age differences, our experi-
ments reveal trends similar to previous research
that show that older adults are more suscepti-
ble to false news and have a greater tendency to
share them (Duke and Whatley, 2021a). This is
mainly due to the difficulty in source monitor-
ing (Brashier and Schacter, 2020) and limited digi-
tal literacy (Moore and Hancock, 2022).

Beyond correctness rates, we analyze multi-
agent interaction responses before / after persua-
sion through persuasion effect analysis.
Using
LIWC dimensions, we measure stance changes be-
tween initial and final responses and find that fe-
male/rural/older participants are more susceptible
to persuasion, while males/younger groups show
greater readiness to act (decisiveness) despite re-
duced confidence. Next, we use three deliberation
metrics inspired by Tessler et al. (2024): (1) emo-
tional change due to persuasion, (2) coverage of in-
teraction content (evidence) in the final responses,
and (3) argument specificity (concreteness of the
final response). We find that rural/younger groups
show higher emotional shift and belief in anecdotes
(higher misinformation vulnerability), urban/older
groups prioritize evidence (but may ignore emo-
tional truths). Females balance emotions and cover-
age, and males exhibit specificity-driven skepticism.
These differences reveal demographic vulnerabil-
ities in LLMs and can guide future demographic-
aware interventions. (See Appendix F.4 for evalua-
tion details using LIWC dimensions.)
Hom(ogeneous) vs Het(erogeneous) groups: We
observe consistent trends in both hom/het groups
following LLM/human persuasion. In LLM per-
suasion, a lower score (+∆CR) suggests that the
correctness of final responses does not increase
compared to the initial responses. This is seen in
4/6 demographics for hom. groups. Conversely,
in human persuasion (−∆CR), a higher score in-
dicates a decline in correctness. Again, this is ob-
served in 5/6 demographics for hom groups. Thus,
correctness of final responses in hom groups does
not improve substantially (for LLM persuasion)
and decreases significantly (for human persuasion)
compared to het groups, showing echo chamber
dynamics (Nikolov et al., 2020; Borah et al., 2025).
Connecting Our Findings to Prior Research.
Röchert et al. (2021) shows that misinformation
spreads more rapidly within hom networks, where
false information is often perceived as “normal”.
Such networks tend to form like-minded cocoons,
referred to as “echo chambers”. Additionally Tan-
war et al. (2024) show that diverse community net-
works show better performance in maintaining ac-
curate information. This aligns with our findings.

Lessons Learned
Our findings revealed the bidirectional impacts
of persuasion on humans and LLMs, alongside
demographic-aware misinformation-based multi-
agent simulations. We demonstrate how demo-
graphic factors shape susceptibility to persuasion
and highlight the potential of demographic LLMs.
These findings offer actionable information for de-
signing targeted demographic interventions.
LLMs for Exploring Demographic Susceptibil-
ity to Misinformation. LLMs offer a preliminary
but useful way to study demographic differences in
misinformation susceptibility. With simple persona
prompts, their responses show positive alignment
with human demographic trends (corr = 0.58),
aligning with previous studies. While not fully
replicating human behavior, refining prompts or
sophisticated training could further improve demo-
graphic simulation, making them a practical tool,
especially when human data is scarce.
5034



===== PAGE 9 =====
Human- and LLM-persuasions can have var-
ied effects. Our results reveal a key asymmetry:
in multi-agent LLMs, while LLM-generated per-
suasion improves correctness, human persuasion
reduces it. This suggests that LLMs may offer
unique advantages in countering LLM misinfor-
mation by generating reliable arguments (Gabriel
et al., 2024; Costello et al., 2024). Future research
should investigate these mechanisms, and optimize
LLM persuasion to complement human reasoning.
LLMs show performance variations in homoge-
neous versus heterogeneous multi-agent settings.
Homogeneous agent groups exhibit lower correct-
ness rates (demonstrating echo chamber effects),
while heterogeneous groups show improved perfor-
mance. This aligns with the Contact Hypothesis
Theory (Allport, 1954), suggesting diverse interac-
tions enhance perspective-taking. Our findings in-
dicate that structured exposure to varied viewpoints
could serve as an effective mitigation strategy.
7
Conclusion
This paper investigated the bidirectional persua-
sion dynamics between LLMs and humans, and ex-
plored their susceptibility to misinformation across
diverse demographics. Using our preliminary anal-
yses, we show that LLMs show potential to sim-
ulate demographic differences and trends in the
context of misinformation. We showed that multi-
agent LLMs exhibit echo chamber behavior when
exposed to misinformation, a phenomenon that can
be mitigated in a heterogeneous setting, consistent
with established psychological theories. Based on
our findings, we share ideas for future research and
open-source our framework, PANDORA.6
8
Limitations and Ethical Considerations
Simplified Representations of Human Demo-
graphics.
Our approach to simulating human de-
mographics using LLMs may oversimplify and not
capture the complexity and diversity of human de-
mographics in the real world. Therefore, caution
is needed when extrapolating large-scale simula-
tions to draw conclusions about human behavior.
Our study shows that while LLMs can simulate
trends similar to those observed in humans with
a moderately strong point-wise correlation (0.58),
a considerable amount of research is still needed
6available
at
https://github.com/MichiganNLP/
PANDORA
before they can fully replicate human thought pro-
cesses, particularly in the context of misinforma-
tion. Additionally, our demographic granularity
remain coarse, focusing only on gender. age and
geographic region, and is limited by Prolific pre-
screens, and these categories only provide a start-
ing point for analyzing demographic differences.
In the future, incorporating a validated rurality in-
dex (Chen et al., 2025) for screening individuals
and/or including socio-economic context would of-
fer deeper insights.
Greater caution is needed when utilizing LLMs
for persuasion.
Linguistic analysis shows that
LLM-generated persuasion is often more effective,
as supported by existing studies. Given its potential
for both positive (Gabriel et al., 2024) and nega-
tive (Danry et al., 2022) outcomes, it is crucial to
approach the use of persuasion with caution and
thoroughly analyze the context before application.
Generalizability across cultures.
Most prior re-
search referenced in our paper is based in the US.
Hence, the generalizability of the findings across
different cultural and geographical contexts re-
mains unclear and requires further investigation.
In addition, we only include participants from the
US for each demographic group in our study. While
their responses align with earlier trends, this limits
the findings and highlights the need for a larger
cross-cultural pool of annotators (Mihalcea et al.,
2025). We encourage future work to analyze demo-
graphic differences across cross-cultural contexts.
Metric Limitations.
In our work, we use correct-
ness rates as a measure of persuasion to determine
susceptibility in various demographics. It directly
encodes the direction of changes with respect to
ground truth. Additionally, it is also robust and
provides a comparable signal across humans and
LLMs (unlike other measures such as confidence,
etc., where calibration could be noisy). This is
in line with our goal to study misinformation sus-
ceptibility, in terms of whether persuasion pushes
decisions toward truth or misinformation. However,
understanding belief formation processes (such as
confidence, reasoning strategies, etc.), although out
of scope for our primary research question, are
important measures of persuasion and therefore,
we suggest future studies to investigate into these
measures.
Stereotypes, Risks and Biases.
We observe that
simulating misinformation in homogeneous groups
5035



===== PAGE 10 =====
leads to an increased spread of misinformation
within multi-agent LLM environments.
There-
fore, our simulation may reinforce existing biases
or stereotypes, particularly if the training data in-
cludes harmful assumptions about specific demo-
graphic groups. There is also the risk that LLMs
could be used maliciously to generate persuasive
content that manipulates vulnerable populations.
Furthermore, it is essential to consider the impli-
cations of using LLMs in sensitive areas such as
political discourse or public health, where misinfor-
mation could have serious real-world consequences.
Ensuring that these ethical considerations are ad-
dressed is crucial to make a responsible contribu-
tion to both AI and society.

degrade or inflate performance; they modulate a

We perform the following qualitative and quantita-
tive analyses to understand the effects of persuasion
during interactions in demographic-aware multi-
agent settings. For our analyses, we specifically
analyze heterogeneous demographic groups. This
approach enables us to identify how persuasion
impacts vary across populations with distinct char-
acteristics, revealing targeted vulnerabilities and
informing tailored interventions.
Structural Analysis. This is conducted by ana-
lyzing text length and readability (Flesch-Kincaid
Grade Level (FK-GL) 8). It reveals distinct linguis-
tic patterns across demographics: Shorter texts
(rural +7.1 words, young +7.8 words) with lower
readability scores (rural 2.86, young -1.23) sug-
gest reliance on simplified emotional appeals, as
conciseness and accessibility may enhance engage-
ment for these groups. In contrast, urban/older de-
mographics exhibit longer texts and higher read-
ability refinement (urban readability +1.82, older
+3.65), aligning with evidence-based persuasion
strategies that prioritize lexical diversity and struc-
tural complexity. Notably, gender differences re-
veal subtle but meaningful patterns: female agents
show marginally lower lexical diversity (female
0.8855 vs. male 0.8890) yet comparable readabil-
ity shifts, while males achieve slightly better final
readability (male -1.17 vs. female -0.49). This
implies that while both genders adapt similarly to
readability demands, males may optimize slightly
more for clarity in persuasive contexts. Magnitudi-
nal differences across demographics are not huge,
however, these findings underscore that text length
and readability are non-neutral dimensions of per-
suasion, showing how different audiences process
different types of appeals.

negative emotion and anxiety from positive
emotion, so higher values indicate a net positive
emotional tone and lower hostility. (3) Cognitive

H
Model Choices, Implementation
Details and Computational Resources
Our model selections across GPT, Llama, and
Qwen are based on three main reasons: (1) ar-
chitectural/origin diversity (OpenAI, Meta, Al-
ibaba), (2) computational feasibility for large-scale
human-LLM experiments, and (3) reproducibility
through open-source model availability. All infer-
ence experiments are conducted with results aver-
aged over three LLM runs. For gpt-35-turbo,
inference is performed using the Microsoft
Azure API 14. The llama-3-70b-instruct15 and
qwen-2.5-72b-instruct16 models are run via
Hugging Face. To ensure focused yet varied text
generation, all models are set with a temperature
of 0.5. For open-source models, top_p is set to
0.9, with do_sample=True, and 4-bit quantization
is applied. Inference for these models is conducted
on an NVIDIA-A40 GPU.
